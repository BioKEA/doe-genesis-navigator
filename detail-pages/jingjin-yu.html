    Introduction

    We are actively seeking an industry partner for the DOE Genesis Mission Phase I RFA (Topic 11-D: AI-Enabled Diagnostics and Remote Handling). Alternatively, we are glad to contribute to projects by offering our extensive expertise in robotic manipulation. 

    Our lab specializes in bridging classical combinatorial algorithms with data-driven AI to solve complex, long-horizon robotic manipulation challenges. Over the past five years, our research has focused heavily on optimal object rearrangement and retrieval in highly constrained, cluttered environments. We have developed state-of-the-art frameworks—such as our Deep Interaction Prediction Network (DIPN) and visual foresight trees—that allow robotic arms to autonomously predict and execute complex prehensile and non-prehensile interactions. We have also pioneered optimal "pick-n-swap" models for high-density lattice rearrangement, alongside robust, real-time 6D object pose tracking systems.

    These capabilities align perfectly with the need for autonomous remote handling in fusion reactors. Our algorithms directly translate to the precision sequencing required for replacing irradiated wall tiles, clearing debris, and navigating cramped vessels.

    We are looking for a hardware or simulation partner (e.g., developers of radiation-hardened telemanipulators or fusion digital twins). We will provide the AI "brains," offering a low-risk pathway to upgrade your systems into fully autonomous solutions.

    Project Idea Summary

    This 9-month Phase I project develops an autonomous robotic manipulation framework for the remote handling and replacement of irradiated plasma-facing tiles in fusion reactors (Topic 11-D). Currently, maintaining fusion vessels relies on slow, human-in-the-loop teleoperation. To achieve a measurable "AI advantage," we will transition this process to full autonomy by adapting our lab’s state-of-the-art algorithms in dense object rearrangement, 6D pose tracking, and visual foresight.

    We will evaluate Phase I success using strict quantitative metrics at the 6-month decision gate, comparing the speed, collision rates, and reliability of our autonomous tile-swapping planner against baseline teleoperation. We will also analyze scaling metrics to show performance improvements as synthetic training data increases, establishing the algorithmic foundation for a Phase II deployment of autonomous, radiation-hardened manipulators to dramatically reduce reactor downtime.
