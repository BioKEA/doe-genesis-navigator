    Introduction

    Prof. Ashutosh Tiwari leads the Nanostructured Materials Research Laboratory at the University of Utah, focusing on plasma-based thin film synthesis and advanced materials characterization. His research emphasizes non-equilibrium processing and structure–property relationships in functional materials, using techniques such as pulsed laser deposition (PLD) to systematically control plasma conditions and study their impact on material properties, including electrical transport, optical behavior, and microstructure.

    In the context of the DOE Genesis Mission, Prof. Tiwari’s group is interested in developing predictive frameworks that connect plasma processing parameters to material outcomes. The approach combines controlled experimentation with data-driven methods, generating high-quality datasets suitable for integration with physics-informed modeling and AI/ML techniques.

    The group seeks to contribute experimental capabilities and data-driven insights toward building scalable, predictive plasma-enabled manufacturing approaches for next-generation microelectronics.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings
    Experimental or test facilities

    We offer plasma-based thin film synthesis using pulsed laser deposition (PLD) with systematic control over processing parameters such as energy, pressure, and growth conditions. This enables generation of high-quality datasets linking plasma conditions to material properties (structural, electrical, optical, magnetic, thermoelectric ). These datasets are suitable for integration with AI/ML frameworks for predictive modeling and optimization of materials processing.

    Subject matter expertise

    Expertise in plasma-based materials processing, non-equilibrium thin film growth, and structure–property relationships in functional materials. Strong background in advanced characterization (XRD, SEM, EDX, TEM, XPS, UV-Vis, transport, magneto-transport) and in correlating processing conditions with material performance. Experience in integrating experimental data with modeling approaches for predictive understanding of materials behavior.

    Relevant Projects

    Project 1: Actinide Materials Discovery through Combinatorial Deposition (DOE Battelle Energy Alliance)
    The DOE Battelle Energy Alliance–funded project “Accelerating Pathways to Actinide Materials Discovery through Combinatorial Deposition” was instrumental in establishing my research program in nuclear and actinide materials. This work enabled development of high-throughput experimental methodologies and sustained collaboration with Idaho National Laboratory. The partnership positioned my group within a national research ecosystem and led to a joint publication in Nature Communications, forming a foundation for future actinide materials research.

    Project 2: High-Throughput Optimization of Copper–Graphene Nanocomposites (DOE)
    This DOE-funded project focuses on enhancing electrical conductivity in copper–graphene nanocomposites using high-throughput experimental approaches. The work emphasizes systematic variation of processing parameters and correlation with electrical and structural properties. It demonstrates integration of experimental design with data-driven analysis for optimizing advanced materials with applications in energy and electronics.

    Project 3: Sustainable Materials and Functional Alloys (DARPA, UU TLO, Wilkes Center)
    Recent projects include DARPA-supported research on sustainable densification of wood, UU TLO–funded work on high-entropy alloys for iodine capture, and climate-focused research on mineral carbonation. These efforts combine materials synthesis, processing, and application-driven design, with emphasis on interdisciplinary collaboration and experimentally driven innovation. In parallel, nuclear fuel–related research has been initiated, resulting in a filed U.S. patent application.

    Project Idea Summary

    This project aims to develop an AI-enabled framework for understanding and optimizing plasma-based processes relevant to advanced microelectronics manufacturing. The focus is on establishing predictive relationships between plasma processing conditions and resulting material properties, addressing a key challenge in achieving repeatable and scalable fabrication at reduced feature sizes and complex geometries.

    The approach integrates controlled experimental data with physics-informed modeling and AI/ML techniques to enable improved process design and optimization. By combining data-driven insights with fundamental understanding of plasma–material interactions, the project seeks to move beyond empirical process tuning toward predictive and adaptive manufacturing strategies.

    The work aligns with the focus area of plasma-enabled microelectronics manufacturing by targeting improved control, reproducibility, and efficiency in plasma-based synthesis and processing.

    We are seeking partners with expertise in plasma physics, modeling, and AI/ML to contribute to the development of predictive frameworks, as well as collaborators with complementary capabilities in semiconductor processing or scalable manufacturing systems.
