Introduction

I am Levent Guvenc, an ASME Fellow and Professor at The Ohio State University, where I co-direct the Automated Driving Lab (ADL). Our expertise in robust autonomous systems directly addresses the cross-cutting goals of the Genesis Mission, spanning ASCR lab automation, AMMTO Smart Manufacturing, FES Fusion Engineering, and NE Autonomous R&D.

While our foundation is in autonomous driving, our core algorithmic capabilities translate seamlessly to embodied AI systems in unstructured, high-stakes environments. We specialize in the mechatronics and control frameworks required for real-time perception, path planning, and remote manipulation under uncertainty. By equipping autonomous mobile platforms with advanced manipulators, we aim to automate complex or hazardous workflows—whether on a smart factory floor, during remote fusion plant maintenance, or inside restricted nuclear hot cells.

Crucially, our lab pioneered the patent-pending Vehicle-in-Virtual-Environment (VVE) methodology. We can adapt VVE into a high-fidelity digital twin framework to train Agentic-AI, optimize robotic designs, and rigorously validate control algorithms prior to physical deployment. Building on our track record of real-world technology transitions—including a key role in the winning U.S. DOT Smart City Challenge—our goal is to deliver a robust, reusable software stack that transforms DOE facilities into adaptive, safe, and highly efficient automated platforms.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

Our core offering is the patent-pending Vehicle-in-Virtual-Environment (VVE) method, a high-fidelity digital twin framework for rigorous testing of autonomous algorithms. We integrate recent work in Safe Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) within VVE to train Agentic-AI in complex, unstructured spaces. This scalable architecture validates learning-based control systems for mobile manipulator platforms, robotic systems in laboratory, manufacturing, or hazardous settings ensuring safety through virtual exploration prior to physical deployment.

AI/ML/Data science expertise

We specialize in the applied mathematics for embodied AI to operate reliably under uncertainty. Our research integrates Deep Reinforcement Learning with Control Barrier/Lyapunov Function low-level controls to ensure "safe exploration" and stability in unstructured environments. Crucially, we have developed novel methods for unknown time delay compensation, ensuring robust performance in real-time control loops where latency is unpredictable. These capabilities enable autonomous platforms to execute precise manipulation and path planning with minimal human intervention.

Experimental or test facilities

The Automated Driving Lab (ADL) at The Ohio State University provides a comprehensive environment for validating learning-based autonomous systems. We utilize advanced hardware-in-the-loop (HiL) and autonomous-vehicle-in-the-loop (AViL) capabilities to bridge the gap between DRL training and real-world deployment. Our facility supports integrating multi-sensor suites with mobile platforms and robotic manipulators, allowing for the empirical evaluation of AI-driven control algorithms under realistic, dynamic laboratory conditions.

Subject matter expertise

We offer deep expertise in mechatronics, robotics, and learning-based control systems. This is anchored by recent projects applying DRL to safety-critical tasks, such as work zone safety and collision avoidance. Our track record includes transitioning autonomous technologies into real-world applications like the U.S. DOT Smart City Challenge. This background ensures a safety-critical approach to systems engineering, combining autonomous mobility with robotic manipulation to create adaptive solutions for the DOE's next-generation facilities.

Relevant Projects

Vehicle-in-Virtual-Environment (VVE) Method for VRU Safety (2023–2025)

Scope of Work: Funded by the National University Transportation Center on Safety and Mobility (Safety 21), this project developed the "Vehicle-in-Virtual-Environment" (VVE) method to evaluate and improve the safety of Vulnerable Road Users (VRUs). The research transitioned from a general VRU focus in its first year to a specific emphasis on bicyclist safety in the second year.

Achievements: The project established a successful methodology for simulating and testing connected and autonomous vehicle (CAV) safety in virtual environments. This work resulted in multiple academic publications.

 

Ford Alliance: Pedestrian Protection Using V2P Communication II (2022)

Scope of Work: This project, part of the Ford Alliance, focused on enhancing pedestrian safety through the use of Vehicle-to-Pedestrian (V2P) communication technologies. The research involved developing communication protocols to alert both drivers and pedestrians to potential collision risks.

Achievements: The research group successfully developed a pedestrian safety application. A significant achievement was the public demonstration of this application at the 2022 ITS World Congress in Los Angeles, California.

 

Parking Assistant for Vehicle with Trailer (2024–2025)

Scope of Work: Sponsored by the Hyundai America Technical Center, this project aimed to develop an automated parking assistant for vehicles towing trailers. The scope included creating path-planning algorithms and advisory systems to manage the complex reverse dynamics of car-trailer combinations.

Achievements: The project led to the development of a hybrid A*-based reverse path-planning algorithm for single-trailer vehicles. Additionally, an advisory system for trailer parking was completed and documented in the SAE International Journal of Advances and Current Practices in Mobility.

While we offer the Automated Driving Lab for initial validation, we seek access to specialized DOE lab or industrial facilities. Access to these specialized environments is essential to validate our algorithms for safe interaction with sensitive scientific instruments and to demonstrate the portability of our cross-cutting software stack.

Subject matter expertise

We seek partners with deep domain expertise in the specific scientific areas of the Genesis Mission. While we provide the robotics and control foundation, we need collaborators who understand the specific constraints and safety protocols of scientific instruments and experimental workflows. This expertise is vital for co-designing "Agentic-AI" logic to ensure that autonomous platforms and manipulators manage experimental workflows correctly and safely interpret scientific results during the discovery process.

Project Idea Summary

This project aims to develop a robust, cross-cutting software and algorithmic stack for autonomous mobile platforms equipped with manipulators to navigate and perform tasks within unstructured, complex laboratory settings. We primarily target ASCR Focus Area A: Advanced Robotics for Dynamic Laboratory Environments, with applications extending to smart manufacturing, fusion engineering, and nuclear R&D.

Our approach leverages the patent-pending Vehicle-in-Virtual-Environment (VVE) digital twin framework to train and validate Safe Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) agents. This methodology enables "safe exploration" by integrating learning-based decision-making with Control Barrier and Lyapunov Function low-level controls. A critical technical advantage we provide is the incorporation of novel methods for unknown time delay compensation, ensuring stable and robust performance in real-time control loops where communication or processing latency is unpredictable.

What we are seeking in a potential partner:

DOE National Labs: We seek access to specialized experimental facilities and real-world scientific workflow data to validate our autonomous platforms/manipulators in high-risk or restricted environments.

Subject Matter Experts: Collaborators in specific scientific domains to co-design Agentic-AI logic, ensuring our autonomous systems can safely and effectively manage specialized experimental workflows and scientific instruments.
