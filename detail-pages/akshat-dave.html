    Introduction

    I am Akshat Dave, Assistant Professor of Computer Science at Stony Brook University, where I direct the Photon Intelligence Lab. My research leverages the physics of light propagation to understand the invisible physical world, by integrating computational imaging and physics-informed AI. Through the Genesis Mission, I am seeking collaborations that bring AI into scientific imaging, sensing, and detector design, grounded in real DOE instrumentation, data, and mission needs.

    My core technical contribution is end-to-end co-design: using differentiable physics simulations and generative AI to jointly optimize hardware (optics, sensors, readout electronics) and algorithms (reconstruction, classification) for scientific instruments. I have demonstrated this approach across LiDAR, cameras, and biological vision systems, with results published in Science Advances, CVPR, SIGGRAPH, and ECCV. I bring these methods to DOE science problems where detector design and data analysis are currently optimized independently, leaving significant performance on the table.

    Relevant Projects

    Project 1: Task-Driven Sensor Design via Neural Design-Space Exploration (2023–2025)
    Developed a framework for automated, task-driven design of imaging sensors by coupling differentiable physics simulations with generative models. Applied normalizing flows to efficiently explore a 6-dimensional LiDAR design space (beam pattern, scanning trajectory, timing resolution, detector layout, wavelength, power) subject to physical and manufacturing constraints. The generative model discovered sensor configurations that outperformed expert-designed baselines on downstream autonomous driving tasks. This work demonstrated that AI-driven exploration of constrained hardware design spaces can yield non-obvious, high-performing designs — a methodology directly transferable to scientific detector optimization for DOE facilities. Accepted at CVPR 2026.

    Project 2: Co-Evolution of Imaging Hardware and Neural Processing (2024–2025)
    Created the first computational framework to simulate the co-evolution of biological vision systems, jointly optimizing optical hardware (eye geometry, lens, retina) and neural processing (visual cortex) for ecological tasks. Using differentiable rendering and evolutionary search, the system discovered that hardware-software co-design yields imaging systems that neither component alone can reach. Validated by reproducing known evolutionary adaptations across 8 animal species. Published in Science Advances 2025. This end-to-end co-design paradigm maps directly onto DOE detector design problems where sensor hardware, readout electronics, and reconstruction algorithms must be jointly optimized. Supported by MIT Generative AI Seed Grant ($70K).

    Project 3: Physics-Informed Computational Imaging for Stress and Material Analysis (2021–2025)
    Developed NeST (Neural Stress Tensor Tomography), a differentiable imaging pipeline that recovers full 3D stress tensor fields inside transparent objects from polarization measurements. The system integrates a differentiable forward model of photoelastic light transport — including birefringence, optical rotation, and multi-wavelength dispersion — with a neural implicit representation, enabling gradient-based reconstruction of quantities previously accessible only through destructive testing. Selected for ACM Showcase at SIGGRAPH, published in ACM TOG 2025. This line of work, including PANDORA (ECCV 2022) for polarimetric scene decomposition and snapshot polarimetric methods (Optics Express 2022), demonstrates our capability to build differentiable physics simulators for complex light-matter interactions and invert them with learned representations — the same methodology needed for differentiable detector simulation in DOE scientific instruments.

    Project Idea Summary

    I develop AI methods for end-to-end co-design of scientific instruments — jointly optimizing detector hardware, readout electronics, and reconstruction algorithms through differentiable physics simulation and generative AI. I am seeking partners with domain expertise, experimental facilities, or data from DOE scientific programs where this co-design methodology can accelerate discovery.

    Relevant Genesis challenge areas include: (14) AI-assisted detector design and data analysis for particle and nuclear physics experiments, including the EIC, DUNE, and collider upgrades; (13) AI-driven accelerator and beamline optimization through differentiable digital twins; (9) AI-driven co-design of microelectronics and on-detector processing for harsh-environment sensors; (8) AI for quantum imaging and sensing — optimizing sensor fabrication and readout for quantum devices; (12) AI-accelerated materials characterization via optimized imaging and measurement systems; (1) end-to-end digital twins for advanced manufacturing with real-time sensor fusion.

    I bring differentiable ray tracing, generative design-space exploration (normalizing flows, diffusion models), and hardware-aware neural architecture methods. I seek partners who bring detector physics, instrument data, facility access, or domain-specific simulation codes to ground this AI methodology in real DOE science problems.
