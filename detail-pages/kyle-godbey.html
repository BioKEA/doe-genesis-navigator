Introduction

I am a Research Assistant Professor at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), where I use microscopic computational models to study nuclear dynamics across scales from the atomic nucleus to neutron stars. My work sits at the intersection of nuclear physics, nuclear astrophysics, and high-performance computing, with a growing emphasis on applying machine learning and uncertainty quantification to extract robust, interpretable predictions from complex nuclear models.

Beyond my research at FRIB, I founded the Advanced Scientific Computing and Statistics Network (ASCSN), a global community dedicated to bridging the gap between computational method developers and domain scientists. ASCSN builds open resources and fosters collaboration around scientific computing, statistical inference, and modern ML techniques making these tools accessible and rigorous for the broader research community.

I am joining the Genesis Mission Consortium to contribute expertise in AI-assisted modeling, uncertainty-aware scientific inference, and community-driven open science particularly as it pertains to advancing basic research, scalable high-performance computing workflows, and the transformative power of AI in workforce development.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI/ML/Data science expertise

I develop and apply machine learning methods to nuclear physics problems, including emulator-based surrogate models for computationally expensive simulations and Bayesian uncertainty quantification for constraining nuclear model parameters. I also build and maintain open educational resources on ML and dimensionality reduction through ASCSN, with a focus on making these methods rigorous and accessible for domain scientists.

Subject matter expertise

Nuclear physicist specializing in microscopic modeling of nuclear dynamics, heavy-ion reactions, and nuclear astrophysics. My work spans phenomena from the structure of exotic nuclei at FRIB to the properties of neutron stars, with particular focus on connecting laboratory measurements to astrophysical observables. I bring deep domain expertise in where AI/ML methods can accelerate — and where they must be carefully validated — in nuclear science.

Other resources/tools

I lead the Advanced Scientific Computing and Statistics Network (ASCSN), an open community platform providing educational resources, forums, and open-source tools at the intersection of computational science, statistics, and applied mathematics. ASCSN serves as a bridge between method developers and domain scientists, and represents a ready-made community channel for disseminating Genesis Mission tools and best practices.

Relevant Projects

Project 1: BAND Framework & BAND-X Cyberinfrastructure (2020–Present) Bayesian Analysis of Nuclear Dynamics | NSF (renewal pending) 

The BAND Framework is a multi-institutional software infrastructure project providing open-source tools for Bayesian uncertainty quantification, model calibration, and emulation in nuclear physics. As a core contributor, I developed and maintained key components of the emulation and calibration machinery, including reduced basis method emulators and Bayesian model mixing tools. These tools have been adopted broadly across the nuclear theory community and underpin several downstream research programs. Building on this foundation, I am leading the BAND-X renewal proposal to NSF — a $5M cyberinfrastructure framework for experimental design in nuclear physics that will integrate automated Bayesian workflows, active learning pipelines, and scalable computing resources. BAND-X is designed to serve as a sustained, community-facing platform connecting theorists, experimentalists, and statisticians around principled uncertainty quantification.

Project 2: STREAMLINE Collaboration — Machine Learning for Nuclear Many-Body Systems (2023–2027) DOE Office of Science | $532K (Phase 1) + $1.386M (Phase 2) | Co-PI

STREAMLINE is a DOE-funded multi-institutional collaboration applying machine learning to nuclear many-body systems. As Co-PI across both funding phases, I have contributed to the development of emulator frameworks for computationally expensive calculations, enabling uncertainty quantification at scales previously inaccessible. Key outcomes include reduced basis method emulators for scattering and bound-state problems, surrogate models for linear response, and emulators for auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo applied to few-body systems and neutron matter — several of which resulted in high-impact publications in Physical Review Letters and Physical Review C. The collaboration spans across more than eight institutions, serving as a model for interdisciplinary AI-augmented nuclear science.

Project 3: Bayesian Framework for Mining Evaluated Nuclear Mass Data (2023–2026) DOE Office of Science | $559,680 | Co-PI

This project develops principled Bayesian frameworks for evaluating, extrapolating, and propagating uncertainties in nuclear mass data — a foundational quantity for nuclear astrophysics, reaction modeling, and stockpile stewardship applications. As Co-PI, I led the development of the Bayesian Mass Explorer (BMEX), a publicly accessible science gateway that allows users to interactively explore model predictions, uncertainties, and Bayesian extrapolations across the nuclear chart. The platform has seen broad community adoption and serves as a template for open, uncertainty-aware nuclear data tools. Methodological contributions include novel Bayesian model mixing techniques using principal component analysis and normalizing flows for posterior reproducibility, with direct applicability to any domain where competing models must be rigorously combined under uncertainty.

Project Idea Summary

Scientific AI only delivers on its promise if the outputs can be trusted and the tools reach the scientists who need them. My work addresses these challenges, and I am seeking partners to build on this foundation through the Genesis Mission.

My domain focus areas fall under Topic 14 (Unifying Physics from Quarks to the Cosmos). My group builds uncertainty-aware emulators, Bayesian calibration pipelines, and model mixing frameworks that make expensive many-body simulations tractable and statistically rigorous. These tools are already deployed in active collaborations in basic and applied nuclear science, and generalize across domains where simulation costs are high.

I am also targeting several focus areas under Topic 18 (HPC Code Curation, Translation, and Development), including trustworthy AI for scientific software and HPC advancement. My group develops and maintains production scientific software and has demonstrated experience deploying cloud-enabled science gateways at community scale.

Underlying both efforts is ASCSN, a global network of researchers in computational and data science that I lead. ASCSN provides a direct channel to support domain scientists in adopting advanced methods, not just learning about them.

I am looking for partners with hard scientific AI problems who need UQ pipelines, teams building HPC or AI platforms who want to build an engaged scientific user community, and groups interested in principled workflows from simulation to inference to deployment.
