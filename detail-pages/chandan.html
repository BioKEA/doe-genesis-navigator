Introduction

I am an Assistant Professor at Iowa State University and a Research Scientist at Ames National Laboratory, where my work focuses on quantum materials, strongly correlated systems, superconducting and topological materials, and the theory of complex electronic behavior in solids. My research sits at the intersection of theory, computation, and phenomenology, with the goal of understanding how new material properties emerge and how they can be predicted and controlled.

In parallel, I am building Scalarity, an effort to develop secure AI systems for scientific reasoning in materials science. The motivation is that modern experiments in spectroscopy, microscopy, and scattering generate large amounts of complex data, but interpreting that data still takes months or years of expert effort. We are developing models that can analyze experimental and simulation data, compare competing physical explanations, and generate testable predictions that can be validated in the lab. Our main tools revolve around physics informed neural networks. 

Because of my access to experimental data and scientific infrastructure through national laboratory collaborations, this work is designed not only to build AI tools, but to create a closed loop between scientific prediction and experimental validation. The broader goal is to accelerate discovery in materials science and eventually extend the same framework to other scientific domains.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

We use supervised learning, generative models, and surrogate models tailored to materials science data. Current work includes training models to predict photoemission and tunneling spectra from synthetic and experimental datasets, and using existing frameworks such as MatterGen and MatterSim as foundations for experiment-specific prediction. Our software stack is primarily Python and PyTorch, with tools for scientific data processing, model training, and secure analysis of spectroscopy and microscopy data.

AI/ML/Data science expertise

Neural Networks for Physical Properties Prediction: This includes using machine learning to analyze spectroscopy and microscopy data, generate synthetic scientific datasets, accelerate theoretical simulations, and connect experimental observations with physical models. I am particularly interested in experiment-specific AI models for techniques such as scanning tunneling microscopy, photoemission spectroscopy, and quasiparticle interference, etc.,  where machine learning can help extract physical meaning from complex data and make testable predictions for new materials. 

Subject matter expertise

Condensed Matter Physics: Superconductivity, Topology, Strong Correlations, Quantum Matter and Materials.

Relevant Projects

1. Theory and prediction of correlated quantum materials (Iowa State University / Ames National Laboratory)

Over the past five years, I have led multiple research projects focused on understanding strongly correlated quantum materials, including superconductors, Mott insulators, topological semimetals, and rare-earth compounds. The scope of this work has included developing theoretical models for electronic structure, collective excitations, and emergent phases, combined with numerical simulations ranging from mean-field calculations to exact diagonalization and Green’s function methods. A major part of the work has involved connecting theory directly to experimental observables such as photoemission spectra, tunneling conductance, and scattering signatures.

A key achievement has been the development of predictive frameworks that explain experimentally observed phenomena in complex materials and generate new experimentally testable predictions. This work has resulted in peer-reviewed publications in leading physics journals, invited talks at international conferences, and new research directions funded through national laboratory and university research programs. The broader impact has been to establish a bridge between fundamental many-body theory and practical interpretation of experimental materials data.

2. AI-guided scientific reasoning for materials data (Scalarity)

I am currently building Scalarity, an effort focused on developing AI systems for scientific reasoning in materials science. The project addresses a major bottleneck in modern materials research: large volumes of spectroscopy and microscopy data are generated routinely, but interpretation remains slow and dependent on scarce expert knowledge. The scope of the work includes training machine learning models on synthetic and experimental datasets to predict and interpret photoemission spectra, tunneling spectra, and quasiparticle interference signals.

Current achievements include machine learning models that can reproduce spectroscopy signatures using synthetic training data and early integration of materials AI frameworks such as MatterGen and MatterSim for experiment-specific prediction tasks. The next phase involves training directly on experimental data from rare-earth materials and generating predictions that can be validated through lab measurements. A key goal is to build secure software that allows research groups to upload data, receive automated analysis, compare theoretical models, and generate experimentally testable predictions within a controlled environment.

3. Experimental-theory collaboration for spectroscopy-driven materials discovery

A major part of my work over the past five years has involved close collaboration with experimental groups using scanning tunneling microscopy (STM), angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES), ultrafast optical probes, and related techniques. The scope has included interpreting experimental data on correlated and topological materials, identifying the physical origin of spectral features, and proposing theoretical models that explain observed behavior.

This work has often involved building minimal models that connect experimental signatures to microscopic mechanisms and suggesting new measurements that can distinguish between competing explanations. In several cases, theory developed through these collaborations has directly informed how experimental data were interpreted and what follow-up measurements were pursued.

Project Idea Summary

Our project focuses on building AI systems for scientific reasoning in materials science, with an initial emphasis on quantum materials and spectroscopy-driven discovery. A major challenge in this area is that modern experiments such as scanning tunneling microscopy, photoemission spectroscopy, and related probes generate complex datasets whose interpretation still depends heavily on slow manual analysis by domain experts. The goal is to develop models that can take experimental or simulation data, identify important physical features, compare possible theoretical explanations, and generate testable predictions for new materials or new experimental conditions.

We are particularly interested in building experiment-specific AI models trained on both synthetic and experimental data, including model-generated synthetic spectra that support theoretical prediction and benchmarking. A key focus is creating workflows that connect data interpretation, model comparison, and experimental validation in a secure environment.

We are seeking partners with expertise in AI model development, scientific machine learning, data engineering, and secure software infrastructure. In particular, we are interested in collaborators who can help strengthen model architecture, training pipelines, synthetic data generation, and deployment of usable tools for research environments.
