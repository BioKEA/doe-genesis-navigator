    Introduction

    The long-term goal of my lab is to determine how plants establish beneficial microbiomes that improve plant growth and reliance. We aim to identify the plant and microbial mechanisms that distinguish beneficial from pathogenic host-microbe interactions. Our current approach relies on identifying harmful and beneficial bacterial secondary metabolites and the plant immune pathways that detect them. We have previously shown that plant immunity can balance beneficial and pathogenic microbial growth via detection of pore-forming toxins. 

    We are currently interested in using an AI-based approach to predict whether uncharacterized bacterial biosynthetic gene clusters are associated with harmful or beneficial plant interactions. Candidate bacterial metabolites produced by candidate biosynthetic gene clusters will be isolated and tested via high-throughput plant assays.

    Relevant Projects

    We have used a combination of RNAseq, RT-qPCR, in vitro and in planta fluorescence-based bacterial competition assays, and plant oxidative burst assays to determine how plants balance beneficial and harmful bacterial growth on roots. We found that two pore-forming toxins, syringomycin and syringopeptin, induce a unique and potent immune response in root tissue, tissue that is typically unresponsive for immunity. We tested purified syrinogmycin to show that it is sufficient for inducing an transcriptomic and physiological plant response and limited pathogen growth. We identified plant genes that were required for the physiological response and showed that syringomycin could not balance harmful and beneficial microbial growth in plants lacking these genes.

    We are working to identify novel bacterial metabolites that induce allow plant immunity to balance beneficial and harmful bacterial growth. We have found that a strain of Pseudomonas fluorescens induces a novel immune response that is not dependent on known plant immune pathways. This strain has predicted biosynthetic gene clusters other syrinogmycin and syringopeptin that may produce secondary metabolites that elicit immunity. We have identified growth conditions for inducing the unknown metabolite and are currently working to isolate and identify this metabolite. Once the metabolite is identified we will knockout its biosynthetic cluster to confirm its effects on plant immunity and bacterial competition.

    Capabilities Seeking
    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    We are interested in training a machine learning model that can predict whether biosynthetic clusters are likely to produce metabolites that have harmful or beneficial interactions with plants. We will need AI/ML/Data science expertise in designing and building this model.

    Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

    Florida State University has a High Performance Compute Cluster. It is possible that this cluster could lack the computational power required for training a model on our bacterial genomic dataset.


     

    Datasets or data infrastructure

    We will need expertise for using an AI-assisted rules-based approached to curate hundreds of bacterial strains into a reliable dataset that can be used for machine learning algorithms.

    Project Idea Summary

    Our goal is to build a model that can predict whether bacterial biosynthetic gene clusters are associated with harmful or beneficial host-microbe interactions. This model will help us significantly increase the rate at which we can identify, test, and isolate novel bacterial secondary metabolites. We will test the effects of these metabolites via our high-throughput plant assays.

    We are seeking a partner with expertise in building machine learning models and AI-mediate database curation.
