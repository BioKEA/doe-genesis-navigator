Introduction

 I am a Professor of ECE at University of Arizona (also affiliated with CS department).  My research interests include network security and privacy, cyber-physical system security, and ML/AI security. I have published more than 150 journal and conference papers, with an h-index of 49. I received the NSF CAREER Award in 2014, the ONR YIP Award in 2016, and several paper awards, including the best paper award from ACM WiSec 2020. I served on the editorial boards of IEEE TMC and TDSC and am currently an associate editor for IEEE TIFS. I was a TPC Co-chair of IEEE CNS 2022. I am a Fellow of IEEE, and a member of ACM.

My expertise is related to the Genesis Mission since cybersecurity is an important part of the Genesis RFA, under topic Area 20: Cybersecurity for AI-driven Science Workflows. I have prior experience of vulnerability finding and defense for machine learning models in autonomous systems, such as autonomous vehicles or drones. The experience is relevant for detecting and mitigating attacks for AI agents in science discovery workflows, such as autonomous labs.

Relevant Projects

1. Funding Agency: DoE (SETO program); Project Title: Achieving Cyber-Resilience for Power Systems using a Learning Model-Assisted Blockchain Framework; Duration: 06/01/2021 - 6/30/2023, Role: Co-PI; Technical Topic Area 5.2: Addressing Cybersecurity Gaps; 

Project Team: Virginia Tech (Lead), Cyber-Physical Security, Inc., Commonwealth Edison, Siemens Corporation, University of Arizona, Utah State University, Washington University, American Center for Mobility. 

Scope of Work: The secure integration and management of distributed energy resources (DER) and power aggregators in the electric grid requires secure communications and a physics-aware Command and Control (C2) strategy. A Blockchain (BC)-based overlay network was developed to provide a security layer for the existing power grid network that mitigates risks in current and legacy network and C2 protocols. By integrating a Model-Assisted Machine Learning (MAML) framework with a Secure Blockchain Overlay Network (SBON) a defense-in-depth strategy was achieved. The MAML framework learned dynamical systems models for individual DERs to detect sensor attacks. We utilized a Digital Twin (DT) to accelerate the learning process for a model resistant to stealthy attacks. The project created DT for PV inverters and BESS. The DTs were coupled with a model-assisted, data-driven learning of DER behavior. Specifically, we evaluated architectures for model-based learning with model-free fine-tuning. Additionally, differential privacy techniques were used to obfuscate data, while still allowing the computation of attack detection results based on obfuscated data. The SBON developed leverages a private permissioned blockchain network orchestrated with the Hyperledger Fabric framework. To connect the cyber world, which orchestrates the blockchain fabric, and the physical world where the power network resides, we developed a system implementation to enable the secure interaction of the physical world and the abstracted blockchain.

Achievements: Final technical report: https://www.osti.gov/biblio/2372923 

2. Funding Agency: National Science Foundation, Title: "SaTC: CORE: Medium: Collaborative: Enforcement of Geofencing Policies for Commercial Unmanned Aircraft Systems", 09/01/2018 - 08/31/2021, PI (in collaboration with Virginia Tech).

Summary: Unmanned Aerial Systems (UAS) offer significant potential but also pose serious security and privacy risks. This project aims to address these critical needs by developing offensive and defensive measures for detecting, identifying, and bringing down UAS that violate controlled/restricted airspace in an automated, controlled, and reliable manner. A three phase approach to thwarting a UAS encroaching on restricted airspace is used: (1) detect the incursion using passive radar that exploits signals of opportunity and radio frequency (RF) communications/emissions from the encroacher itself; (2) deploy one or more defender UAS, capable of learning the motivations and behaviors of the encroacher, to pursue the UAS and gain and maintain proximity to it even should the encroacher take evasive action; and (3) have the defender UAS launch offensive measures that manipulate/compromise the sensor and actuation signals the encroacher relies on to stay airborne.

Achievements: https://www.nsf.gov/awardsearch/show-award/?AWD_ID=1801402&HistoricalAwards=false

Project Idea Summary

Project Title: "Safety and Security Guardrails for AI-Driven Autonomous Labs"

High-level summary: This project addresses cybersecurity risks and "logical hallucinations" in AI-scientists or AI-driven autonomous labs, which can lead to real-world hazards. We propose a Root-of-Trust framework that utilizes formal logic to enforce safety specifications. The system ensures safety through Global Verification and Synthesis to block "trap states" and Constrained Decoding to filter unsafe tokens during real-time LLM inference. Ultimately, this research provides mathematical guarantees for AI reliability in high-consequence environments, directly supporting the GENESIS mission. It address topic 20 in the GENESIS RFA (Cybersecurity for AI-driven Science Workflows, and focus area C: Real-Time Attack Detection and Mitigation for AI Models).
