Introduction

Chang-hee Won is a Professor in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering and the Director of the Control, Sensor, Network, and Perception (CSNAP) Laboratory at Temple University, Philadelphia. He was a visiting researcher at the University of Pennsylvania and a Fulbright Distinguished Chair. He is a topic editor of the IEEE Sensors Journal. Currently, he is actively guiding various research projects funded by the National Science Foundation, the Pennsylvania Department of Health, and the Department of Defense. He has published over 120 peer-reviewed articles and has received multi-million dollars in research funding as a principal investigator from industry, state, and federal funding sources. His research interests include robotics, tactile sensing, optimal control theory, dynamic sensing systems, and reinforcement learning. He is working on the development of a novel robotic digital twin method that will autonomously handle energy samples.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

Traditional robotic manipulator control relies on constructing a mathematical model of the system and then designing a controller based on that model. However, this approach depends on precise mathematical modeling and offline computation of control parameters. We have capabilities in integrating, sensing, computing, and controlling robots so that they can operate autonomously in complex, uncertain, real-world laboratory environments. To achieve this, we develop and utilize reinforcement learning (soft actor critic) and digital twin methodologies.

Subject matter expertise

My primary research interest is in control theory with robotic applications. Most recently, this focus has shifted toward robotic manipulator control using reinforcement learning. My main theoretical research interest is in the development of statistical control theory. Then we solve statistical optimal control problems using reinforcement learning without mathematical model of the system. This has applications in robotic control. We also have expertise in the development of tactile sensing systems and sensor integration.

Capabilities Seeking
Experimental or test facilities

We are developing an autonomous robotic system for energy sample handling and preparation. This includes autonomously retrieving samples from storage, mixing reagents in precise ratios, loading and unloading centrifuges, filtration systems, or reactors, and labeling and tracking vials throughout the workflow. We are seeking an energy laboratory that can test and utilize such a robot and the developed methodologies. We are also seeking researchers who have experience in energy sample handling.

Project Idea Summary

The primary objective of this project is to design and implement a Robotic Digital Twin equipped with a novel artificial intelligence-based control method for autonomous energy laboratory operation. Here, we define the Digital Twin as a fully integrated, bidirectional data flow between a physical object and a digital object. This is more than a computer simulation working in conjunction with a physical system; it is closely related to control theory. Together, these innovations will provide a transformative approach to autonomous, real-time operation of laboratory equipment, advancing the state of the art in robotics and AI for energy research. The target application of this technology is a robot for sample handling and preparation. This includes autonomously retrieving samples from storage, mixing reagents in precise ratios, loading and unloading centrifuges, filtration systems, or reactors, and labeling and tracking vials throughout the workflow. We are seeking collaborators who have experience in energy sample handling and laboratory facilities that need such a robotic system.
