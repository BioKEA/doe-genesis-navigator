    Introduction

    My research focuses on applying quantum sensing techniques to fundamental questions in nuclear and particle physics. We develop experiments with individual atoms and molecules containing rare nuclei, which are produced artificially in specialized laboratories and are available only in extremely small quantities.

    Relevant Projects

    Project 1: Laser Spectroscopy of Short-Lived Radioactive Molecules at CERN-ISOLDE (2020–present)

    Scope: Led an international collaboration to develop a novel experimental approach for spectroscopy of short-lived radioactive molecules at CERN's ISOLDE facility, using the Collinear Resonance Ionization Spectroscopy (CRIS) experiment. The program targeted radium monofluoride (RaF), predicted to offer unprecedented sensitivity to symmetry-violating effects relevant to the matter-antimatter asymmetry. Work encompassed production of radioactive molecules in quantities below 10⁻⁸ g, multi-step laser manipulation, and interpretation alongside quantum chemistry and nuclear theory collaborators.

    Achievements: Performed the first-ever laser spectroscopy of a short-lived radioactive molecule (Nature 581, 396, 2020), measured RaF isotope shifts (PRL 127, 033001, 2021), developed a precision laser cooling scheme (Nature Physics 20, 202, 2024), and achieved the first observation of nuclear magnetization distribution in a molecule (Science, 2025). Recognized with the APS Freedman Award (2022), IUPAP Young Scientist Prize (2022), Sloan Fellowship (2023), and Popular Science "Brilliant 10" (2023).

    Project 2: Design and Commissioning of the RiSE Experiment at FRIB (2020–present)

    Scope: Supported by the DOE Early Career Award, led the design, fabrication, and commissioning of the Resonant ionization Spectroscopy Experiments (RiSE) at the DOE Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), in collaboration with FRIB scientists. The project involved developing a high-sensitivity multi-step laser ionization apparatus for studying rare isotope atoms and molecules at the limits of nuclear existence, integrated into FRIB's BECOLA facility.

    Achievements: RiSE is now fully operational — the first laser spectroscopy system of its kind at FRIB — enabling virtually background-free measurements on isotopes produced at rates as low as a few ions per second. The system has already been successfully used in experiments studying neutron-deficient aluminum isotopes down to the proton drip line and very exotic silicon isotopes, demonstrating its reach into previously inaccessible regions of the nuclear chart. Multiple approved experimental proposals target exotic actinide systems for nuclear structure and fundamental symmetry studies.

    Project 3: Nuclear Structure of Exotic Nuclei via Charge Radii and Electromagnetic Moments (2020–2025)

    Scope: Led a broad experimental program at CERN-ISOLDE measuring charge radii and electromagnetic moments of exotic isotopes across multiple elements using collinear laser spectroscopy. Coordinated international teams of 20–30 researchers, developed detection schemes with laser spectroscopy, and interfaced results with ab initio and density functional nuclear theory calculations.

    Achievements: Produced high-impact discoveries including: abrupt structural change at magic number N=82 in indium (Nature 607, 260, 2022); challenge to the magic character of N=32 in potassium (Nature Physics 17, 439, 2021); first microscopic description of odd-even staggering in copper (Nature Physics 16, 620, 2020); charge radii of nickel (PRL 128, 022502, 2022), scandium (PRL 134, 182501, 2025), silicon (PRL 132, 162502, 2024), and indium isotopes elucidating the doubly magic character of ¹⁰⁰Sn (Nature Physics 20, 1719, 2024). This program produced 50+ papers over five years.

    Project Idea Summary

    We are developing an AI-native framework that integrates quantum sensing protocols, physics-informed digital twins, and adaptive measurement control to dramatically enhance the sensitivity of experiments with single, trapped molecules aiming to give answer to major open questions in our understanding of the universe such as the origin of the matter-antimatter asymmetry of our universe, the properties of dark matter, and the possible existence of new undiscovered fundamental particles and forces.  The symmetry-violating signals are extraordinarily weak, buried in noise that is orders of magnitude larger. Recent theoretical developments indicate that specific quantum protocols, accelerated by AI, can achieve provably superior sensitivity for exactly this class of problems. The AI system will: (1) design and adaptively select optimal QEC sensing protocols in real time; (2) build and maintain a physics-informed digital twin for real-time noise tracking; (3) design quantum logic pulse sequences for molecular state preparation and readout; and (4) validate all protocols on an NV-center testbed before molecular-ion deployment.
    What we are seeking in a national lab partner:
    We are looking for a DOE national laboratory or industry partner who can contribute one or more of the following:
    * Trapped-ion quantum error correction or quantum control expertise — experimental experience implementing QEC protocols, real-time feedback control, or AI-driven calibration on trapped-ion platforms.
