Introduction

Professor of Mechanical Engineering and Materials Science and Engineering with expertise in materials behavior in extreme environments, including creep-fatigue, microstructure–property relationships, and advanced manufacturing. My work focuses on developing predictive, physics-informed frameworks for materials performance and qualification, with growing interest in integrating AI, high-throughput experimentation, and in-situ data to accelerate deployment of materials in energy systems.

Relevant Projects

DOE NEUP Project: Creep–Fatigue Interaction and Qualification of AM 316H Stainless Steel for High-Temperature Cyclic Service

Scope:
Investigated creep–fatigue (CF) behavior in wrought and additively manufactured (LP-DED) 316 stainless steels to establish a technical basis for accelerated testing and qualification of materials for high-temperature nuclear service. The work combined experimental testing (550–700 °C, multiple dwell conditions) with evaluation of life prediction models used in nuclear design codes (time fraction, ductility exhaustion, and stress-modified ductility exhaustion approaches).

Achievements:

Generated new CF datasets for both wrought and AM 316H, enabling direct comparison of deformation and failure mechanisms. 

Demonstrated that conventional accelerated CF test protocols (short dwell times) do not capture the most damaging interaction modes, requiring revised testing strategies. 

Identified that ductility exhaustion-based models improve life prediction accuracy, particularly when accounting for non-damaging viscous strains. 

Showed that AM 316H exhibits non-conservative life predictions under existing models, despite similar monotonic properties to wrought materials. 

Impact:
Established the need for microstructure-aware, physics-informed qualification frameworks for AM materials, directly supporting deployment of advanced materials in nuclear energy systems.

 

DOE NETL UTSR Project: AI-Enabled Quality Assurance for Additive Manufacturing

Scope:
Developed an integrated, physics-informed and data-driven framework for qualification of LPBF Inconel 718 by linking process conditions, in-situ monitoring, defect formation, and fatigue performance. The work combined advanced diagnostics, multimodal data fusion, and mechanistic materials characterization to establish robust process-structure-property relationships for AM components.

Achievements:

Designed and deployed a high-speed, high-fidelity in-situ monitoring system (STWIP) capable of capturing melt pool temperature and morphology at up to 100,000 fps, overcoming emissivity limitations of conventional sensing and enabling physically meaningful thermal measurements. 

Developed a process-agnostic, deep learning framework that quantitatively predicts pore presence, size, count, and volume fraction across AM regimes, achieving strong predictive accuracy even under highly stochastic process conditions. 

Integrated in-situ monitoring with XCT-based defect characterization and mechanical testing, enabling direct linkage between melt pool signatures and defect populations. 

Established new understanding of fatigue-critical behavior by demonstrating that fatigue life in AM IN718 depends on a competition between pore severity and crystallographic microstructure, with a transition from defect-controlled to microstructure-controlled crack initiation at near-full density. 

Quantified the limitations of traditional qualification metrics, showing that monotonic properties alone cannot predict fatigue performance in AM materials. 

Impact:
This work established a comprehensive, multiscale qualification framework combining in-situ sensing, machine learning, and mechanistic fatigue understanding.  It provides a pathway toward real-time certification and digital qualification of AM components for energy systems.
