    Introduction

    Gerald Quon, Ph.D., is an Associate Professor at the University of California, Davis, whose research focuses on developing machine learning methods to understand how genomic variation gives rise to biological function and phenotype. His work sits at the intersection of artificial intelligence, genomics, and functional biology, with the long-term goal of building predictive models that connect genotype to phenotype across diverse biological systems.

    Gerald's group develops deep learning and statistical approaches that integrate large-scale genomic and functional datasets to model regulatory programs, genetic perturbations, and evolutionary variation. His research has contributed methods for analyzing high-throughput sequencing data, modeling gene regulation, and designing CRISPR-based perturbation experiments.

    For the Genesis Mission RFA, Quon is interested in collaborations that connect large-scale genomic modeling with experimentally tractable systems in microbes, plants, or model organisms. His goal is to develop generalizable AI frameworks for predicting and engineering biological function from genome sequence, leveraging the computational infrastructure and experimental capabilities of the national laboratories.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings
    AI models, tools, or software

    I develop machine learning models that predict biological phenotypes directly from genome sequence. My group builds deep learning frameworks for genotype-to-phenotype prediction that integrate genomic, functional genomics, and perturbation datasets across species. We also develop tools for modeling regulatory DNA, analyzing high-throughput CRISPR screens, and learning representations of biological conditions and environments to improve predictive modeling of gene function, cellular behavior, and evolutionary variation.

    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    I develop machine learning methods for modeling complex biological systems from large-scale genomic and functional datasets. My expertise includes deep learning for sequence modeling, representation learning, multimodal data integration, and probabilistic modeling. I build AI frameworks that connect genomic variation to molecular and cellular phenotypes, with experience applying ML to high-throughput perturbation data, regulatory genomics, cross-species comparative analysis, and predictive modeling of gene function and cellular responses.

I am also interested in collaborations that provide data infrastructure for managing, standardizing, and sharing high-throughput perturbation and phenotyping datasets suitable for training large-scale predictive models.

    Experimental or test facilities

    I am interested in experimental platforms that enable systematic measurement of phenotypes across many genetic variants and environmental conditions. This includes high-throughput microbial growth and fitness assays, CRISPR perturbation screens, automated phenotyping platforms, and facilities for large-scale comparative experiments across strains or species. Such systems would allow testing and validation of AI model predictions by experimentally probing genotype–phenotype relationships in controlled, scalable settings.

     

    Project Idea Summary

    My project aims to develop artificial intelligence methods that improve our ability to connect genome sequence to biological function and phenotype across diverse organisms. Despite the rapid growth of genomic data, predicting how genetic variation affects traits, fitness, or cellular behavior remains a major challenge. Current approaches are often limited to narrow systems or specific experimental settings and do not generalize across organisms, environments, or perturbations.

    The focus of this effort is to explore new AI and machine learning frameworks capable of learning generalizable representations of genomic variation and biological context. The goal is to create models that can integrate genome sequence with experimental measurements across many conditions, enabling improved prediction of phenotypes and helping guide future experiments and engineering efforts.

    I am particularly interested in collaborations that provide experimentally tractable systems where genotype–phenotype relationships can be measured at scale. This could include microbial systems, plants, or model organisms with large collections of strains or perturbations measured across diverse environmental conditions. Access to high-throughput phenotyping platforms, large genotype–phenotype datasets, or experimental facilities capable of validating model predictions would be especially valuable.
