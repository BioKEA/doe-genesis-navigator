Introduction

The University of Louisville is a metropolitan R1 research institution with engineering talent, industry‑ready graduates, and strong applied research capabilities. The J.B. Speed School of Engineering combines small‑college attention with large‑university resources and requires three co‑op rotations for every student, ensuring a steady pipeline of engineers with real-world experience who can contribute immediately to industry projects.

UofL’s research infrastructure supports advanced manufacturing, automation, energy systems, and materials innovation. The Additive Manufacturing Institute of Science and Technology (AMIST) delivers end‑to‑end expertise in metal and polymer 3D printing, digital manufacturing, and rapid prototyping for industrial applications. The Conn Center for Renewable Energy Research focuses on scalable solutions in solar energy, energy storage, advanced materials, and efficiency technologies, supported by experienced researchers and strong partnerships with private industry. The Louisville Automation & Robotics Research Institute (LARRI) brings multidisciplinary capability in industrial automation, human‑robot collaboration, autonomous systems, and intelligent manufacturing.

Together, our faculty, researchers, and co‑op‑trained students work with industry partners to accelerate innovation, solve technical challenges, and deploy practical, production‑ready technologies.

Relevant Projects

Project 1: Advanced Electrochemical and Photochemical Systems (DOE, NSF, EFRC)

Over the past five years, our team has led multiple federally funded programs advancing electrochemical CO₂ conversion and solar-driven hydrogen production. As PI on a $1M DOE project, we developed nonaqueous electrochemical CO₂-to-ester pathways, producing eight journal publications and a patent. Through an NSF CAREER award, we pioneered tandem semiconductor microparticles for unassisted solar water splitting, generating six publications and training eight undergraduate researchers. As Co-PI and Thrust Leader in a DOE Energy Frontier Research Center, we led efforts in reactive CO₂ capture using ionic liquids and imidazolium carbenes, contributing to six publications. Collectively, these programs established foundational mechanisms, materials, and reactor concepts enabling next-generation electrochemical and solar-fuel technologies.

Project 2: High-Performance Alloy Design and Additive Manufacturing (NASA, DOE, DoW)

Our materials team executed major programs in alloy development and metal additive manufacturing. For NASA, we designed and printed a custom Hf-based refractory high-entropy alloy system for extreme-temperature aerospace environments. In a DoW SBIR project, we developed and validated a W-based alloy system through powder production, printing, and testing. Under a DOE initiative for industrial furnace applications, we identified an optimized RHEA chemistry for heating elements and fabricated components via AM. These efforts advanced alloy-design workflows, experimental validation platforms, and qualification methods for high-temperature systems.

Project 3: Smart Manufacturing for Cement Production (DOE–CESMII, ARGOS USA)

We led two DOE–CESMII projects focused on optimizing energy-intensive cement production through predictive modeling, data analytics, and machine learning. The first phase created an AI-enabled process model for full-scale kilns, achieving simulated energy reductions up to 25% and measured reductions of ~8%. This work received a CESMII/DOE Innovation Award. The second phase expanded the model to integrate both kilning and calcining processes, improving accuracy and identifying additional opportunities for savings. Together, these projects demonstrated the potential for >15% energy reduction and set the foundation for next-generation smart-manufacturing control architectures.

Project 4: Robotics, Human–Machine Interaction, and Multiscale Manufacturing (NSF KAMPERS, Nexus MRI, NNCI)

Across three major NSF programs, our robotics team advanced multiscale manufacturing, soft robotic interfaces, and human–robot collaboration. As co-PI on the $20M KAMPERS project, we co-developed structural electronic materials, biodegradable 3D-printed robotic systems, and modular tactile sensors—contributing to more than 20 journal and 30 conference papers and enabling the formation of UofL’s LARRI robotics institute. Through the $2.3M Nexus MRI project, we built a robotic platform for micro-to-macro fabrication of microsystems, microrobots, and multimaterial devices. As co-PI on the NSF NNCI Multiscale Node, we expanded national capabilities in nanoscale-to-mesoscale fabrication, enabling integration of dense sensor arrays for human-robot interaction and emerging materials research.

Project Idea Summary

Our project portfolio centers on advancing next‑generation energy, materials, and manufacturing technologies through integrated experimental, computational, and intelligent systems approaches. We work across the clean‑energy spectrum—developing new electrochemical and photochemical pathways for converting CO₂ and sunlight into valuable products, designing high‑performance alloys for extreme environments, and leveraging advanced additive manufacturing to rapidly prototype and validate novel material systems. Our teams also focus on improving industrial processes through smart‑manufacturing strategies that combine physics‑based models, sensors, automation, and machine learning to reduce energy intensity and enhance process reliability.

In parallel, we lead major efforts in robotics, multiscale fabrication, and human–machine systems. These include creating robotic platforms for micro‑ to macro‑scale manufacturing, developing new sensing and control interfaces for collaborative robotics, and integrating AI to accelerate materials discovery and system optimization. Across all areas, our projects share common themes: enabling more efficient and sustainable production systems, coupling advanced materials with intelligent manufacturing, and building integrated experimental and digital frameworks that can transform industrial, energy, and robotic technologies.
