    Introduction

    I am a researcher who works on modeling and algorithms for autonomous laboratories and facilities, having over a decade of experience in the field. I work on Bayesian methods, decision-making under uncertainty, with a focus on designing and evaluating algorithms to orchestrate agents within realistic constraints occurring in day-to-day research and development. I am an associate professor at the University at Buffalo, and am joint appointed as an applied mathematician in Brookhaven National Laboratory, and serve as both an academic or DOE national lab partner for Genesis proposals.

    Relevant Projects

    Autonomous Nanoparticle Synthesis via Closed-Loop AI: Led modeling and decision-making algorithm development for a closed-loop autonomous synthesis platform integrating Gaussian process surrogates, Bayesian optimization, and real-time multiscale characterization. Supervised model validation and uncertainty-aware exploration across high-dimensional chemical spaces, enabling efficient search over approximately 19,000 conditions with 365 experiments and achieving precise control of nanoparticle size and polydispersity while revealing new structure–synthesis relationships. Published in Journal of the American Chemical Society; DOI: 10.1021/jacs.5c03875.

    AlphaFlow: Reinforcement Learning–Driven Autonomous Multi-Step Chemistry: Led modeling and decision-making algorithm development for a reinforcement learning–guided self-driving laboratory capable of autonomous discovery and optimization of multi-step nanoparticle synthesis pathways in high-dimensional, data-sparse environments. Supervised the design and assessment of sequence-aware decision policies, belief models, and forward simulation strategies enabling efficient navigation of reaction spaces exceeding 40 dimensions, with real-time adaptation to experimental feedback. The system autonomously identified novel reaction sequences outperforming conventional protocols, achieving improved shell growth, material quality, and experimental efficiency while demonstrating clear advantages over Bayesian optimization in high-dimensional settings. Published in Nature Communications (2023); DOI: 10.1038/s41467-023-37139-y.
     

    ML-Guided Diameter Control of Carbon Nanotubes: Developed and implemented machine learning–driven decision policies for autonomous optimization of single-walled carbon nanotube (SWCNT) synthesis, targeting precise diameter control through closed-loop experimentation. Designed a Gaussian process–based Bayesian optimization framework using an Expected Improvement acquisition policy, augmented with a support vector machine classifier to learn feasible growth regimes and avoid non-productive experiments. Enabled efficient navigation of a high-dimensional synthesis space defined by temperature and precursor partial pressures, identifying distinct optimal conditions for closely spaced nanotube diameters (~0.91 nm and ~1.06 nm) within fewer than 100 experiments. Demonstrated selective growth with up to ~35% concentration in targeted diameter ranges and revealed nontrivial, regime-dependent synthesis pathways driven by reductant–oxidant balance. Published in npj Computational Materials (2021); DOI: 10.1038/s41524-021-00629-y.

    Project Idea Summary

    The project focuses on developing a unified framework for autonomous laboratories that integrates multi-physics simulations with multi-modal experimental data to enable decision-making over heterogeneous actions and long time horizons. The central challenge is to perform computationally efficient multi-modal data assimilation under uncertainty while coordinating diverse experimental and simulation modalities within a single decision loop. This includes learning surrogate models that fuse heterogeneous data streams, handling partially observed and asynchronous measurements, and optimizing sequences of actions when objectives are uncertain, delayed, or evolving. The work targets challenge areas in autonomous experimentation, data fusion, and long-horizon decision-making for complex physical systems.

    I am seeking partners with complementary capabilities in experimental platforms (for example, fluidic labs, materials synthesis, or in situ characterization), multi-physics simulation environments, and real-time data infrastructure. Ideal collaborators would bring expertise in integrating hardware with software control stacks, standardized data pipelines, or domain-specific modeling, enabling joint development of closed-loop systems that couple simulation, experiment, and decision-making at scale.
