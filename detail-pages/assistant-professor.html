Introduction

I am Jiayu Peng, Assistant Professor in the Department of Materials Design and Innovation at the University at Buffalo, SUNY, and I am seeking partners for Genesis Mission teams at the intersection of AI, materials, energy, and advanced manufacturing. My group develops AI-enabled, physics-informed approaches for materials discovery across chemically and structurally complex systems, combining machine learning, atomistic simulation, materials theory, and characterization-informed modeling to accelerate how new materials and processes are identified, understood, and translated.

I am eager to connect with DOE national laboratories, user facilities, industry partners, and experimental research teams building multidisciplinary Genesis efforts. I can contribute strengths in AI model development, atomistic and disorder-aware materials modeling, structure-property-performance prediction, integration of simulation with characterization, and closed-loop high-throughput computational discovery.

I am particularly interested in teaming on efforts that connect AI with hypothesis generation, high-throughput computation, multimodal synthesis and characterization, experimental validation, and closed-loop discovery. My expertise is especially relevant for teams working on AI-enabled materials discovery, materials processing, autonomous laboratories, catalyst and electrochemical materials development, critical-mineral-relevant materials systems, and manufacturable energy materials.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

I offer AI models and software workflows for physics-informed materials discovery, including theory-embedded, symmetry-aware, and alchemical graph neural networks, machine-learning interatomic potentials, differentiable inverse design, and an in-house autonomous high-throughput DFT/ML platform for active learning, screening, and closed-loop model refinement across energy materials, catalyst and reactive materials, critical-mineral-relevant systems, and manufacturable materials platforms.

AI/ML/Data science expertise

I bring AI/ML and data-science expertise in geometric deep learning, atomistic and disorder-aware modeling, active learning, inverse design, and integration of multimodal synthesis, characterization, and simulation data. My work spans dataset curation, representation learning, high-throughput DFT/ML workflows, interpretable and physics-informed modeling, and closed-loop discovery for AI-enabled materials discovery, materials processing, autonomous labs, and critical-mineral-relevant materials systems.

Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

My group is enabled by a distinctive multi-tier compute ecosystem spanning local RTX 4090/5090 workstations, the University at Buffalo (UB)’s Center for Computational Research (CCR), the Empire AI Consortium, and NSF ACCESS resources. UB is home to Empire AI, a $500M New York State investment in an AI computing center. CCR provides a leading academic HPC and research-cloud environment with >30,000 CPU cores, advanced GPUs, high-speed networking, and expert support. We also hold NSF ACCESS allocations on Delta and DeltaAI for large-scale AI/ML and automated high-throughput DFT.

Datasets or data infrastructure

My group has a mature in-house data infrastructure built around a tens-of-thousands-line Python codebase that automates high-throughput DFT workflows and large-scale AI model training. Backed by an SQL database with a custom schema and Django-based object-relational management, this platform has already automated numerous DFT calculations. It is in routine use for developing, curating, and deploying new datasets for model training, evaluation, and closed-loop materials discovery, and is fully available at project start.

Subject matter expertise

I can provide subject matter expertise spanning catalysis and electrocatalysis, electrochemical interfaces, materials stability and surface reconstruction, chemically disordered and high-entropy materials, atomistic thermodynamics and kinetics, and AI-enabled materials discovery. My work also connects computation with multimodal characterization and mechanistic interpretation across energy materials, catalysts, and reactive materials, critical-mineral-relevant systems, and manufacturable materials platforms, with emphasis on interfacial behaviors and structure-property-performance relations.

Relevant Projects

Selected relevant project experience (including collaborative and pre-faculty research efforts)

1. AI for disordered and high-entropy materials discovery

I have led research on AI methods for chemically ordered and disordered materials, with emphasis on symmetry-aware graph neural networks, alchemical graph neural networks, and machine-learning interatomic potentials for compositionally complex systems. The scope of this work has included high-throughput DFT data generation, model development for ordering-dependent property prediction, and closed-loop strategies for identifying stable and synthesizable materials. Key achievements include the development of symmetry-aware GNNs for crystalline ordering, physics-informed descriptors for cation ordering in multicomponent oxides, and alchemical ML methods for differentiable atomistic modeling. This work provides a strong foundation for Genesis-relevant efforts in AI-enabled materials discovery, critical-mineral-relevant materials systems, and manufacturable materials platforms.

2. AI-enabled catalyst discovery and accelerated materials screening

My research has also focused on AI-driven catalyst discovery across multimetallic, oxide, and interfacial materials systems. The scope of this work has included surrogate modeling, optimization-guided screening, and the integration of mechanistic understanding with data-driven discovery. I have contributed to efforts such as machine-learning-enabled atomistic understanding of oxide catalysts, optimization-driven compositional and structural exploration, predictive catalyst stability modeling, and contributions to community-level roadmaps on transforming catalysis with AI. Together, these efforts demonstrate experience highly relevant to Genesis themes in catalyst discovery, autonomous laboratories, and AI-guided materials development.

3. AI for electrochemical interfaces, materials stability, and surface reconstruction

A third major area of my recent project experience has been the use of AI, atomistic simulation, and characterization-informed modeling to understand dynamic interfaces and materials durability under reactive conditions. The scope of this work has included electrocatalyst stability, surface dissolution and reconstruction, electrochemical interface thermodynamics, and the interpretation of complex characterization data from evolving materials surfaces. Key achievements include published design principles for catalyst stability, predictive modeling of electrocatalyst surface reconstruction, and AI-accelerated simulations of electrochemical interfaces. This experience is directly relevant to Genesis opportunities involving correlation-to-understanding, multimodal data integration, and the development of interpretable AI frameworks that connect computation, experiment, and mechanistic insight.
