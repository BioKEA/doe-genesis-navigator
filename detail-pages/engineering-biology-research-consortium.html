Introduction

The Engineering Biology Research Consortium (EBRC) is a non-profit, public-private partnership dedicated to bringing together an inclusive community committed to advancing engineering biology to address national and global needs. We showcase cutting-edge research in engineering biology, identify pressing challenges and opportunities in research and application, articulate compelling research roadmaps and programs to address them, and provide timely access to other key developments in engineering biology. Driven by member-led working groups, we focus on Research Roadmapping, Education, Security, and Policy & International Engagement. We also have extensive experience as organizers and facilitators of workshops on a range of topics, from the technical meetings to trainings on ethical, legal, and social implications of engineering biology research.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
Subject matter expertise

EBRC has deep technical knowledge of the intersection of engineering biology and artificial intelligence, recognizing them as innovation engines for a robust bioeconomy and research ecosystem. Our staff have participated in several fora focused on AIxBio security issues and authored white papers on risk mitigation of biological design tools and biological data governance. As a member-driven organization, we draw heavily on the expertise of our 237 academic and over 20 institutional members to inform our work.

Other expertise

EBRC is renowned for convening experts from across disciplines and sectors like academia, industry, and government and facilitating dialogue between technical and non-technical experts. Our proven track record of designing and facilitating structured workshops have produced high impact reports and supported effective collaborations. We also maintain strong relationships with executive branch departments and federal researchers that help communicate the needs of researchers to policymakers and help researchers understand how policy changes impact their research.

Relevant Projects

Engineering Biology for Space Health (NNX16AO69A)

EBRC collaborated with the NASA Translational Research Institute for Space Health (TRISH) to facilitate and lead the production and publication of a technical research roadmap for space health. The roadmap describes goals and milestones for new tools and technologies that can improve human health and well-being during space exploration missions and help solve societal challenges here on Earth, including providing food and water access and sustainability, expanding equitable precision medicine, and enabling and ensuring resources to support life and control the local environment, particularly when those resources are limited. The roadmap was created through the contributions of academic, industry, and government leaders, experts, exceptional trainees, and diverse stakeholders representing the convergence of engineering biology, cell and molecular biology, biomedicine, materials, aerospace, and social science fields. The roadmap will be used to spur innovation and collaboration and as a tool for TRISH and NASA to guide programmatic and investment decisions.

Enabling Quality, Measurable Synthetic DNA Sequence Screening

EBRC, in partnership with National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), convened stakeholders to i) articulate the structure and properties of a sequence of concern database(s), with consideration for security and access controls, and the screening tools that will be needed for its use by the nucleic acid synthesis industry; ii) develop a test dataset—or describe the characteristics of a test dataset—that can be continuously updated, to support standards for, and conformity assessment of, effective nucleic acid synthesis screening; and iii) support and/or advise NIST, the DNA synthesis community, and/or the Customers and Users of synthetic DNA in the implementation and use of new frameworks, tools, and/or best practices.

Engineering Biology Metrics and Technical Standards for the Global Bioeconomy

EBRC, with partners at the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), Imperial College London, and the National University of Singapore (NUS), and supported by Schmidt Futures, have published a report focused on determining the engineering biology metrics and technical standards needed to accelerate the global bioeconomy. The report summarizes the key findings that emerged from global stakeholder discussions, pulling together common themes and identified needs that arose across Europe, Asia, and the Americas. Within six key areas — data standards, metrology and metrics to quantify biological processes, scale-up and scale-out, lexicon and terminology, metrics and standardization for sustainability assessments, and standards to enable use of biomass feedstocks — opportunities are identified for focused activities to develop technical standards and metrics that will enable enhanced performance across the bioeconomy: improving reproducibility, supporting continued scale-up, and accelerating commercialization and industrial growth. A series of non-technical areas are also identified and explored, including: training and education on standards and metrics, engagement with the public and improvement of public perception and trust, regulatory clarity, and biosafety and biosecurity.

Capabilities Seeking
AI/ML/Data science expertise

To complement our expertise using multi-stakeholder convenings to advance standards development across sectors of engineering biology, we seek partners with AI/ML and data science expertise to define technical requirements for machine-readable biological data, both current and forward-looking, ensuring standards remain relevant as AI model architectures evolve. Experience across scientific disciplines navigating dataset harmonization challenges for model training is valued to enable standards that support interdisciplinary data interoperability.

Datasets or data infrastructure

To complement our expertise developing roadmaps that identify technical challenges and opportunities in engineering biology, we seek partners managing biological datasets or data infrastructure to: characterize strengths and limitations of current datasets; provide practitioner expertise on barriers to adoption across institutions and sectors; and serve as testbeds for real-world validation as standards are developed. Partners with data relevant to biomolecular science, biodesign, biomanufacturing process scale-up, or biological pathways to CMM are especially encouraged.

Project Idea Summary

Scaling the biotechnology revolution and securing domestic critical minerals supply share a common bottleneck: the absence of standardized, AI-ready biological datasets. Engineering biology generates vast data, but fragmentation and incompatible formats block AI-scale model training. Without agreed-upon standards for AI-ready biological data and incentive mechanisms to drive adoption, the AI advantage the Genesis Mission envisions will remain out of reach. 

As a non-profit renowned for convening cross-sector experts, EBRC is uniquely positioned to lead multi-stakeholder consensus-building on AI-ready biological data standards across several focus areas: biomolecular science, biodesign, process scale-up and integration, and biological pathways to CMM. Leveraging a decade of experience advancing engineering biology to address national and global needs, EBRC aims to address three interconnected gaps: interoperability standards for multi-omics and microbial datasets, governance mechanisms to incentivize adoption, and public-private alignment. 

As a partner, EBRC contributes a convening infrastructure that produces technical consensus, established stakeholder relationships, and a record of context-specific deliverables that advance policy implementation. We seek partners with AI/ML or data science expertise to define technical requirements for machine-readable data and partners managing biological datasets or data infrastructure relevant to these focus areas.
