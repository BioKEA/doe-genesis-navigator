Introduction

I am an Assistant Professor of Astronomy and computational astrophysicist San Diego State University, an R1 public research university. I work at the intersection of physics, high-performance computing (HPC), and AI-driven scientific discovery.

My research focuses on building physics-first, differentiable simulation and inference frameworks that enable us to learn directly from data using real models. I develop GPU-native, JAX-based tools for large-scale scientific inference, with applications ranging from stellar physics to population-level modeling with survey data (e.g., Rubin LSST). This includes open-source infrastructure for differentiable forward modeling, Bayesian inference, and scalable, end-to-end modern scientific pipelines that spans HPC, scientific machine learning, inverse problems, and uncertainty quantification. I also care deeply about training the next generation of scientists to think computationally and physically at the same time.

I’m especially interested in collaborations that push toward AI-native scientific workflows where simulation, inference, and learning are integrated into a single system. That includes embedded-physics inference, differentiable modeling, agentic or AI-assisted research pipelines, and scalable methods for extracting physical insight from complex, high-dimensional data. I’m looking to partner with with DOE labs and industry teams who want to build real scientific infrastructure — not just models, but systems that change how science.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

I develop open-source, physics-first AI tools for scientific discovery, including differentiable simulation and inference frameworks (e.g., stellax, jaxstro). These systems integrate forward modeling, probabilistic inference, and automatic differentiation to enable gradient-based learning of physical parameters directly from data. All tools are GPU-native, scalable, and designed for real scientific workflows — not black-box ML.

AI/ML/Data science expertise

Expertise in scientific machine learning, inverse problems, and Bayesian inference at scale. I specialize in combining physical models with modern ML methods (JAX, NumPyro) to enable efficient, uncertainty-aware inference in high-dimensional systems. Experience includes HMC/NUTS, hierarchical modeling, differentiable programming, and building AI-enabled pipelines for large survey datasets.

Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

Experience developing and deploying GPU-native scientific workflows on modern HPC systems, including AMD and NVIDIA architectures. Work leverages JAX for portable execution across CPUs, GPUs, and TPUs, with scaling via vmap/pmap for large ensembles. Active use of HPC clusters for simulation, inference, and large-scale data analysis.

Datasets or data infrastructure

Extensive experience generating and analyzing large-scale simulation datasets, including radiation magnetohydrodynamics and star cluster simulations. I develop synthetic observation pipelines that transform simulations into realistic, instrument-aware data products with noise, selection effects, and systematics. This enables end-to-end workflows from simulation to inference, bridging the gap between theory, data, and scalable scientific analysis.

Subject matter expertise

Computational astrophysics, star formation, stellar physics, and radiation magnetohydrodynamics, with emphasis on connecting simulations to observations. My work focuses on extracting physical insight from complex systems using first-principles models, particularly in regimes where traditional methods fail due to degeneracies, hidden structure, or scale.

Relevant Projects

Project 1: Differentiable Star Cluster Inference Pipeline (jaxstro)

Scope: Developed an end-to-end, GPU-accelerated, differentiable pipeline for inferring stellar cluster parameters from photometric data. The system integrates forward modeling, survey-aware rendering, and Bayesian inference (HMC/NUTS) using JAX.

Achievements: Built a fully differentiable inference framework enabling joint estimation of age, metallicity, IMF, and binary populations with exact gradients. Demonstrated order-of-magnitude speedups over traditional methods and established a new paradigm for physics-based inference at survey scale.

Project 2: Radiation Magnetohydrodynamics Simulations of Star Formation (STARFORGE / ORION2)

Scope: Led and contributed to large-scale RMHD simulations of star formation and stellar feedback, incorporating gravity, radiation, magnetic fields, and turbulence. Work involved development and application of HPC simulation codes and analysis pipelines.

Achievements: Produced high-resolution simulations connecting physical processes to observable structure in star-forming regions. Contributed to widely used community simulation frameworks and enabled quantitative comparison between simulations and observations through synthetic diagnostics.

Project 3: Simulation-to-Observation Pipeline for Synthetic Data and Inference

Scope: Developed pipelines that transform simulation outputs into realistic, instrument-aware synthetic observations, including radiative transfer, noise models, and selection effects. Designed workflows to connect simulations directly to inference-ready datasets.

Achievements: Enabled direct comparison between theory and observations, reducing systematic biases in parameter inference. Established end-to-end workflows linking simulation, data generation, and statistical inference—bridging a key gap in scientific data analysis.

Project Idea Summary

I am interested in developing AI-native scientific workflows for embedded-physics inference in complex, nonlinear systems, aligned with the “AI for Scientific Reasoning” challenge area.

The core idea is to build end-to-end differentiable pipelines that integrate simulation, data generation, and inference into a single system. These workflows enable joint inference of system parameters and embedded physics (e.g., transport coefficients, closure relations, microphysics parameters) directly from data using gradient-based methods.

This approach addresses a key limitation in current scientific computing: physics inside models is typically fixed rather than inferred, leading to biased or incomplete results at scale. By combining implicit differentiation, hierarchical Bayesian inference, and GPU-accelerated computing, we aim to make these parameters jointly learnable in large, high-dimensional systems.

I am seeking partners with expertise in DOE-relevant application domains (e.g., plasma physics, fluid dynamics, materials, or energy systems), as well as teams with access to large experimental or observational datasets. Collaborations with DOE National Labs and industry partners working on HPC, AI infrastructure, or scientific data systems are especially of interest.

The goal is to build transferable, open, and scalable infrastructure for AI-driven scientific discovery across domains.
