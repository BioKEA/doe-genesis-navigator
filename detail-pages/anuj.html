Introduction

I am an Associate Professor in the Department of Computer Science at Virginia Tech, where I develop scientifically grounded AI methods in the rapidly growing field of Knowledge-guided Machine Learning (KGML) for complex systems across environmental, biological, and physical sciences. The goal of KGML is to integrate domain knowledge such as governing equations, physical laws, process-based models, and phylogenetic structure into machine learning to enable interpretable, data-efficient, and generalizable models. We focus on advancing AI for Science through physics-informed machine learning, hybrid-physics-AI models, equation discovery, and multi-scale multi-physics modeling, with applications ranging from fluid dynamics and geophysics to multi-omics and ecological systems. Some of the recent thrusts of our group include building hybrid digital twins that combine simulations with data-driven models, domain-specific multimodal foundation models, as well as modular and compositional AI systems that integrate foundation models, scientific simulators, and symbolic reasoning tools. We are also exploring agentic AI workflows for closed-loop scientific discovery and generative approaches for inverse design in scientific domains such as biology. Overall, KGML aims to transform AI into a principled partner in scientific reasoning by reducing reliance on large datasets while enabling robust prediction, control, and discovery across complex energy-relevant systems.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

We have developed phylogeny-guided AI models for organismal biology that encode evolutionary structure into representation learning methods, interpretable AI, and generative AI. Our work includes agentic AI workflows and multimodal foundation models for lake dynamics under sparse, noisy, and incomplete data. We have also designed knowledge-guided architectures for multi-phase flow and PDE systems, integrating physics constraints, simulations, and data for robust, multiscale modeling.

AI/ML/Data science expertise

My core expertise is in Knowledge-Guided Machine Learning (KGML), which embeds scientific knowledge into AI models to enable data-efficient, interpretable, and generalizable learning. We develop methods spanning physics-informed loss functions, knowledge-guided AI architectures and agentic workflows, and phylogeny-guided representation learning methods. Our work includes building multi-modal scientific foundation models (for lakes), joint forward–inverse modeling (for geophysics), generative AI (for organismal biology and solving PDEs), and hybrid physics–AI digital twins (for ecology).

Relevant Projects

Imageomics (NSF HDR Institute Grant)
As part of the NSF HDR Institute, we have established a new field of Imageomics, where images serve as a primary modality for discovering biological traits through knowledge-guided machine learning (KGML). The scope of this project includes developing methods that integrate evolutionary information, phylogenetic structure, ontologies, and multimodal biological knowledge into vision and vision-language models for trait discovery and evolutionary analysis. A key achievement is the development of Phylogeny-guided Neural Networks (Phylo-NN) that uses knowledge about the tree of life to enable interpretable feature learning across evolutionary scales. We have also introduced phylogeny-guided diffusion models that generate biologically consistent organismal images, and HComP-Net, which learns hierarchical prototypes to support interpretable reasoning and hypothesis generation about trait evolution. More recently, we developed TaxaAdapter to adapt pretrained diffusion models for biologically consistent generation. Our work demonstrates how KGML can transform representation learning, interpretable AI, and generative AI into a scientifically grounded and discovery-driven framework.

LakeFM (NSF Macrosystems Biology + NAIRR Pilot Award)
Supported by an NSF Macrosystems Biology grant and the NAIRR Pilot Award, we have developed LakeFM, the first scientific foundation model for forecasting water quality across lakes and reservoirs at continental scales. This work was recognized at the national level, including an invitation to present at the White House during the NAIRR program launch. The scope of this project is to fundamentally rethink foundation models in science by integrating observational data with process-based models, simulations, and governing equations during both pretraining and inference. Other contributions in this project include the development of Physics-guided Neural Networks (PGNNs) that incorporate constraints such as energy balance and monotonicity and Modular Compositional Learning (MCL), where neural modules correspond to distinct physical or biogeochemical processes, enabling reusable, interpretable, and compositional modeling across lakes.

KGML Foundations (NSF CAREER Project)
My NSF CAREER project advances the theoretical and algorithmic foundations of knowledge-guided machine learning (KGML) for integrating diverse forms of scientific knowledge into AI models. The scope spans forward modeling, inverse modeling, and hybrid modeling across domains such as fluid dynamics, geophysics, and environmental systems. A key contribution is the Generalized Forward-Inverse (GFI) framework for subsurface imaging, which unifies prior deep learning approaches by modeling both physical parameters and observations in latent spaces and learning bidirectional mappings between them. This framework enables systematic analysis of existing methods and supports the development of new, more general formulations for seismic inversion. We have also developed methods to analyze and stabilize loss landscapes, including adaptive balancing of competing physics-based and data-driven objectives, and resampling strategies to mitigate failure modes in PDE learning. Additionally, we introduced PRISMA, a conditional diffusion neural operator that embeds PDE residuals directly into model architectures using spectral attention, enabling fast, stable, and gradient-free inference with significant gains in efficiency and robustness.

Project Idea Summary

I am interested in contributing to multiple projects, particularly in AI for Scientific Reasoning (Topic 19) and related areas, with a focus on developing modular and compositional AI models grounded in scientific knowledge. The core idea I want to explore is to leverage the paradigm of Knowledge-Guided Machine Learning (KGML) in advancing AI for Science by incorporating governing equations, process-based models, phylogenetic structure, and physical constraints into the design, training, inference, and evaluation of AI models. For example, we can develop Agentic AI workflows where modular components represent distinct scientific processes or domain-specific foundation models, and are composed in scientifically meaningful ways to enable prediction, control, and discovery in complex emergent systems. A key goal is to enable data-efficient, interpretable, and generalizable AI for multi-scale, multi-physics systems.

I am seeking partners with strong domain expertise across Earth and environmental systems (climate, hydrology, biogeochemistry), subsurface and geophysics (seismic imaging, fracture dynamics), biotechnology (multi-omics, microbiomes, evolution), materials science, fluid and energy systems (multiphase flow, thermal systems, plasma/fusion), advanced manufacturing, and autonomous laboratories. I am particularly interested in deep cross-disciplinary collaborations to integrate domain knowledge into AI systems and advance both AI methodologies and scientific applications.
