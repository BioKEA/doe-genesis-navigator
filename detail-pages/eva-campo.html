Introduction

At Campostella Research and Consulting  we believe that the data that results from research, be it public or private, has value across space and time.  When data are managed properly—including provision of descriptive metadata, robust storage, persistent identifiers, and usage restrictions and licenses as appropriate—they can be repurposed and combined with other data in ways that lead to new discoveries and increase the return on investment.  Publicly funded research data are equivalent to a public good, where multiple uses do not conflict with each other and usage does not diminish the value of the data.  Public goods and research data are not necessarily free to provide or to use, yet the collective benefits outweigh the costs of provision and use. 

Campostella can assist and advise organizations, teams, and individuals in how to improve data management governance and infrastructure.  We base our work on the NIST Research Data Framework (RDaF, 2023), which describes the research data lifecycle and identifies the roles and responsibilities that individuals have within the research data ecosystem.  We can guide organizations through a self-assessment using the RDaF as a maturity rubric, identifying those areas needing improvement and identifying the positive outcomes.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI/ML/Data science expertise

We have experience working with projects at institutional, national, and international scale, within government, academic, and private sector environments.  Our expertise, knowledge, and resources in research data help organizations to:

Treat data as a disruptive technology that opens doors to new business and research opportunities,

Realize the value of growing a culture of data sharing, and facilitate the creation of that culture,

Identify and implement solutions that leverage metadata at the highest level, and

Protect intellectual property and data integrity.

Datasets or data infrastructure

In the context of the physical sciences, we have led the development of NIST’s public data repository, managed the Standard Reference Data Collection, and led the Research Data Framework  project. We have developed standards for data discovery and interoperability in the international metrology community. We led the Virtual Astronomical Observatory, an effort co-funded by the NSF and NASA, to build a distributed but federated system for data management used, e.g. supporting data access for the Hubble and James Webb missions.

Subject matter expertise

A combined 30+ years experience in research data management, metadata standards, interoperability, and reproducibility (FAIR principles) having advised and consulted the National Academy of Science, European Physical Science Consortia, Research Data Alliance, CODATA, and facilites funded by the NSF in the realms of Physical and Chemical Sciences, Biology and Pharma, and Computing infrastructure. Former members of the OSTP/NSTC Subcommittee on Open Science and the Board on Research Data and Information at the National Academies.

Project Idea Summary

We propose a cross-cutting role within the Genesis ecosystem: a data and translation layer that enables:
- Provenance-rich data management across experimental and computational workflows
- Interoperability between heterogeneous data sources and AI models
- Readiness assessment and qualification support for emerging materials
- Continuous feedback loops between AI predictions and experimental validation

Our approach builds on established frameworks such as the NIST Research Data Framework (RDaF), emphasizing lifecycle data stewardship, governance, and trust. By harmonizing data and enabling AI-driven decision-making at the process level, this infrastructure supports the transition from discovery to deployment.

This capability is not tied to a single domain but is designed to operate across multiple Genesis teams, providing a shared foundation for accelerating innovation. It is particularly relevant for non-equilibrium processes, advanced manufacturing, and complex energy systems where data fragmentation currently limits progress.

By embedding this infrastructure within the Genesis Mission, the United States can ensure that its investments in AI and scientific discovery yield tangible outcomes: qualified materials, scalable processes, and deployable technologies that strengthen energy security and industrial competitiveness.
