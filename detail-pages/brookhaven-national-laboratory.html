Introduction

The U.S. Department of Energy's (DOE) Brookhaven National Laboratory is a multidisciplinary research institution located on Long Island in New York. Its 3,000+ employees and 7,000+ annual facility users and visiting scientists work to accelerate pathways to scientific discovery and technological innovation that transform the world. Brookhaven is overseen and primarily funded by DOE's Office of Science and is managed by Brookhaven Science Associates, a partnership between Stony Brook University and Battelle and six core universities: Columbia, Cornell, Harvard, Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Princeton, and Yale. Brookhaven researchers and industry partners will take on key roles in tackling Genesis Mission science and technology challenges related to manufacturing advanced microelectronics, operating and securing the electric grid, and exploring connections between the smallest building blocks of matter and the largest structures in the universe, among other efforts. Brookhaven will provide critical contributions through its DOE Office of Science user facilities, including the National Synchrotron Light Source II, Center for Functional Nanomaterials, Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider, future Electron-Ion Collider, Accelerator Test Facility, along with the Lab's Computing and Data Sciences facilities and resources.

Brookhaven Lab hosts one of the world’s largest scientific datasets. The Lab has high-bandwidth, federated access to this dataset. Brookhaven offers curated datasets from large-scale experiments in Nuclear and High Energy Physics to help unlock cross-experimental AI discovery potential.

Experimental or test facilities

Brookhaven has major scientific facilities for experiments and testing, including the National Synchrotron Light Source II, Laboratory for Biomolecular Structure, Center for Functional Nanomaterials, Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider, Accelerator Test Facility, NASA Space Radiation Lab, and Quantum Networking facility. Relevant Projects

1. Scientific Imaging Beyond Current Limits
Modern materials and biological systems are often too complex to study fully with current imaging tools alone. Scientists frequently work with incomplete or noisy data, which makes it harder to see important patterns. One Brookhaven project uses AI to improve how X-ray images are reconstructed and analyzed. By training AI models on scientific data, researchers can extract useful information faster and with much less input than traditional methods require. In some cases, AI can reconstruct X-ray images 10 times faster, reduce the data needed for 3D imaging by a factor of 10, and cut image analysis from weeks of human work to minutes of computing time. This allows researchers to interpret results while experiments are still underway, helping to speed discovery, improve productivity, and reveal hidden relationships in materials and biological systems that would otherwise take much longer to uncover.

2. Powering the Future of Biotechnology
Drug discovery is often slow because it depends on many manual experiments and limited high-quality data. AI has great potential in this area, but it needs strong datasets. This Brookhaven project combines AI, robotics, and X-ray science into a continuously operating discovery platform designed to generate that data at scale. The system has already increased throughput by 50% in fiscal year 2025 compared with FY 2024, reaching more than 70,000 samples per year, with a path to another 50 percent increase. The larger goal is to create richer biological datasets that can train better AI models and speed the search for new medicines and delivering new treatments for cancer and other diseases sooner. 

3. AI-Accelerated Grid Expansion and Interconnection of Large Loads
The electric grid must connect major new power demands, including large data centers, much faster than it can today. Existing planning tools are too slow to evaluate the many possible locations and reliability scenarios involved. This project uses an AI system called GridFM to speed up those studies dramatically. It can quickly screen all possible connection points, test how the grid would respond to equipment failures, and identify the best options. In one example, a large synthetic Texas grid required about 10 billion power flow simulations. Traditional methods would take roughly 20 years of calculations, while the AI approach cuts that to about two months. AI can help utilities and planners make faster, smarter decisions about where to add new electricity demand, reducing costs and improving reliability.

4. Transforming Materials Discovery with AI
Discovering new materials for energy, electronics, and quantum technologies takes time because researchers must test many material combinations one by one. Mapping a single sample library can take months. This project uses AI to make that process much faster and more efficient. The system coordinates two X-ray experiments at once, with one helping guide the other, while AI analyzes results in real time and selects only the most useful next measurements. In one example, a task that normally required about 16,000 measurements and two months could be completed with roughly 400 measurements in two days, while still achieving over 99 percent accuracy. Faster materials discovery can help advance batteries, electronics, and other technologies that depend on improved performance and lower cost.
