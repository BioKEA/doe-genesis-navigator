    Introduction

    Dr. Tao Wen is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Earth and Environmental Sciences at Syracuse University. His research develops integrated geochemical, hydrologic, and AI-enabled data science approaches to understand and predict how fluids, gases, and dissolved elements move through Earth and energy systems. His group combines field observations, laboratory geochemistry, and machine learning to address problems related to subsurface fluid migration, groundwater quality, elemental cycling, energy development, and environmental monitoring.

    As it pertains to the Genesis Mission, Dr. Wen’s expertise is especially relevant to AI-accelerated scientific discovery from complex, multi-source Earth and energy datasets. His work includes building predictive and interpretable machine learning models for geoscience applications, integrating physical and geochemical constraints into data-driven workflows, and using geochemical tracers (including noble gases, stable isotopes, and major/trace element chemistry) to infer subsurface processes that are otherwise difficult to observe directly.

    His group is interested in partnerships that connect AI, scientific computing, and geoscience to advance DOE-relevant challenges, including subsurface characterization, fluid and reactive transport, energy and environmental monitoring, critical resource exploration, and scalable workflows for scientific data integration and prediction.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings

    AI-enabled modeling and analysis workflows for Earth and energy systems, including machine learning models for prediction, classification, and pattern discovery from complex environmental and geochemical datasets. Experience developing reproducible Python-based pipelines for feature engineering, model training, interpretation, and visualization, with emphasis on physically informed and interpretable approaches for subsurface characterization, fluid tracing, and environmental monitoring.

    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    AI/ML and environmental data science methods for hydrogeochemistry and water resources: geospatial analysis, time-series modeling, uncertainty quantification, and machine learning workflows. Experience building predictive models that combine field observations, laboratory measurements, and domain knowledge to improve forecasts and decision support.

    Datasets or data infrastructure

    Environmental datasets and reproducible pipelines: water quality and quantity time series, noble gas and hydrogeochemical observations, curated metadata, and scripts for cleaning, feature extraction, and geospatial visualization.     Hydrogeochemistry and noble gas research laboratories (HANDS and NEST) at Syracuse University, supporting field sampling, laboratory analysis, and data integration for subsurface and environmental systems. Capabilities include noble gas mass spectrometry (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe), stable isotope analysis, ion chromatography, and optical emission spectrometry. Facilities support controlled sampling, tracer-based process inference, and integration with AI-enabled workflows for subsurface characterization, fluid tracing, and environmental monitoring.

    Subject matter expertise in subsurface fluid systems, hydrogeochemistry, noble gas geochemistry, groundwater quality, water–rock interaction, and elemental cycling across natural and energy-impacted environments. Relevant application areas include hydrocarbon and gas migration, produced water and brine tracing, basin-scale fluid mixing, environmental monitoring, and geochemical process inference from sparse, noisy, or multi-source datasets.

    Relevant Projects

    • AI-Discovered Watershed Intelligence for Freshwater Sustainability (2024–present)
    Developing physically informed machine learning frameworks to improve prediction and process understanding of freshwater systems across scales. This work integrates hydrologic, geochemical, and environmental datasets to identify controlling factors, improve interpretability, and support AI-enabled scientific discovery in complex Earth systems.

    • Predicting Riverine Alkalinity and Salinity Fluxes Using AI (2024–2026)
    Built interpretable machine learning models to predict freshwater alkalinity and salinity fluxes across U.S. watersheds under changing climate and human pressures. Combined hydroclimatic, geospatial, and geochemical data to improve predictive performance and reveal dominant environmental controls. Outcomes include scalable workflows and peer-reviewed publications.

    • DeCODER: Democratized Cyberinfrastructure for Open Discovery (NSF CSSI, 2022–2026)
    Leading development of open, community-driven cyberinfrastructure for reproducible Earth and environmental data science. Scope includes data integration, metadata standardization, and collaborative computational workflows that improve access, reuse, and interoperability of scientific data and tools across teams.

    • Groundwater Quality and UOGD Impacts in Southwestern Pennsylvania (HEI Energy, 2021–2026)
    Co-led interdisciplinary efforts to evaluate groundwater and surface water quality near unconventional oil and gas development. Combined targeted field sampling, geochemical tracers, and statistical analyses to distinguish localized anthropogenic signals from natural background variability and improve environmental process attribution.

    • Noble Gas Tracers for Subsurface Fluid Migration and Hydrocarbon Systems (2020–present)
    Developing and applying noble gas and isotopic tracer approaches to understand fluid origin, mixing, and migration in subsurface systems. This work integrates laboratory measurements and geochemical interpretation to improve characterization of basin-scale fluid processes relevant to hydrocarbon systems, subsurface resources, and energy-related Earth science applications.
