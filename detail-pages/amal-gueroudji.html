Introduction

I am a researcher at the Math and Computer Science Division at Argonne National Laboratory. My work broadly spans data management, HPC/AI systems, and agentic workflows, with a focus on enabling scalable, reliable, and transparent scientific computing. My interests include scalable vector databases, data and workflow management for complex HPC and AI pipelines, and the use of provenance to support trustworthy scientific AI and reproducibility across distributed environments. I have contributed to memory-efficient, scalable training and system-level optimizations, as well as end-to-end and in-situ provenance capture—tracking agent reasoning, planning, model configurations, and tool interactions. My work also explores integration challenges across heterogeneous systems and frameworks, along with performance monitoring and online feedback mechanisms, supporting adaptive and scalable agentic and scientific workflows.

Relevant Projects

Performance and Provenance for Distributed and Hybrid Workflows (RECUP). I developed a layered observability stack for Dask Distributed that captures fine-grained task, communication, and I/O metadata and streams it via Mofka for real-time analysis. By correlating scheduler, worker, and storage behavior, this system provides audit-ready insights into performance variability and enables reproducibility at scale on systems like Frontier and Aurora. This directly supports the Genesis mission by providing the provenance and observability foundation needed for agentic workflows to reason about execution, diagnose variability, and ensure trustworthy, reproducible science. Informed by the layered profiles and provenance data, I designed distributed and memory-efficient strategies for training spatiotemporal GNNs using Dask Distributed, enabling multi-GPU scaling on large datasets without partitioning. The resulting system preserves model accuracy while improving computational and memory efficiency and has been integrated into PyTorch Geometric Temporal. This supports the Genesis mission by enabling scalable machine learning within scientific workflows, forming the foundation for intelligent, adaptive agents that can learn from and optimize complex computational pipelines.

Data Management for Agentic Systems (TIDES – VectorDB on HPC). In the TIDES project, I investigate how vector databases such as Qdrant can serve as high-performance, queryable “AI memory” layers on HPC systems, analyzing their scalability, memory efficiency, and support for RDMA and GPU-aware execution. This work establishes a methodology for evaluating and adapting vector data systems to leadership-class architectures and for integrating them with simulations, instruments, and workflows. It supports the Genesis mission by providing the memory layer that enables agents to store, retrieve, and reason over heterogeneous scientific data in a scalable, auditable, and real-time manner.

Toward Trustworthy Agentic AI in Scientific Workflows (Diaspora)
I extended the concept of resilience in workflows to cover the new types of failures we can have in agentic systems, such as hallucinations, prompt injection, and so on. I defined a new framework that, instead of focusing on a single agent's error, extends the resilience and reliability to the whole workflow. It proposes mechanisms for trustworthy agentic workflows by formalizing agentic faults (e.g., hallucinations, unsafe actions, and retrieval errors) and designing provenance-aware control strategies such as policy gates, pre/post-conditions, and rollback mechanisms. This work also contributes to a unified safety–security–reliability model and a provenance-centric architecture for tracking and governing agent interactions across workflows. It advances the Genesis mission by ensuring that autonomous agents operate in a transparent, auditable, and controllable manner, transforming them into reliable instruments for scientific discovery.

Project Idea Summary

We develop scalable vector databases and provenance systems for HPC environments to support agentic, non-agentic, and hybrid scientific workflows. Our work addresses two key challenges: (1) bringing HPC principles (e.g., performance, data locality, and scalability) into AI systems to enable efficient, large-scale scientific workflows, and (2) improving the reliability of agentic systems through integrated provenance collection, real-time inspection, and auditability mechanisms.

We are particularly focused on building an “AI memory” layer for workflows—where vector databases, provenance, and runtime signals are tightly coupled to support reasoning, reproducibility, and control. We are seeking partners in domain science to provide use cases to test, validate, and refine these systems in real-world settings, ensuring they are tailored to scientific requirements and can effectively support discovery at scale.
