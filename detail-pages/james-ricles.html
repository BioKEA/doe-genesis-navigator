    Introduction

    James M. Ricles is the Bruce G. Johnston Professor of Structural Engineering at Lehigh University and Director of the NSF-supported NHERI Lehigh Experimental Facility in addition to the Advanced Technology for Large Structural Systems Research Center. He is the Editor-in-Chief for Engineering Structures. His background integrates structural dynamics, nonlinear mechanics, and multi-physics system behavior, with early-career experience in offshore energy systems at Exxon Production Research Company and extensive leadership in large-scale experimental and computational research for civil and energy infrastructure.

    His research has pioneered real-time hybrid (cyber-physical) simulation of multi-physics systems, enabling the coupling of physical experiments with advanced numerical models to capture complex system behavior under extreme conditions. More recently, his work has advanced AI-enabled simulation frameworks, integrating neural network surrogate models into real-time multi-physics cyber-physical environments to represent fluid–structure interaction and other coupled phenomena with high fidelity and computational efficiency. He is actively seeking to partner with National Laboratories and industry leaders to develop AI methods that overcome the computational bottlenecks of conventional CFD and fluid–structure interaction, enabling rapid, high-fidelity characterization of flow-induced phenomena and component response for next-generation energy systems.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings
    AI models, tools, or software

    Our team develops AI-enabled models and software for real-time simulation of multi-physics systems, focusing on fluid–structure interaction (FSI) and thermal–hydraulic behavior. Neural network surrogates, trained on high-fidelity CFD, replace computationally intensive solvers within a cyber-physical simulation framework. Embedded in real-time multi-physics simulation, these tools enable rapid, high-fidelity prediction of flow-induced phenomena and component response, overcoming CFD bottlenecks and supporting digital twins for next-generation energy systems.

    Experimental or test facilities

    Our team leverages large-scale experimental infrastructure at Lehigh University, including the NSF-supported NHERI Lehigh Experimental Facility at ATLSS. The facility enables real-time hybrid (cyber-physical) simulation by coupling physical components with numerical models to evaluate multi-physics behavior under realistic loading. Capabilities include multi-directional servo-hydraulic actuation, strong floor and reaction wall systems, and high-speed control with sub-millisecond synchronization, supporting dynamic testing with fluid–structure, soil–structure, and thermal effects.

    Relevant Projects

    AI-Enabled Real-Time Hybrid Simulation of Fluid–Structure Interaction Systems for Energy Applications (2023–2026)
    Developed a cyber-physical simulation framework integrating neural network (NN) surrogate models within real-time hybrid simulation (RTHS) to replace CFD in fluid–structure interaction (FSI) problems relevant to energy systems. Demonstrated accurate prediction of vortex-induced vibration and flow-induced fatigue with ~77× computational speedup and <2% error, enabling real-time digital twins for advanced reactor cooling systems and high-performance thermal-fluid infrastructure.

    AI-Enabled Soil–Foundation–Structure Interaction for Energy and Infrastructure Systems (2022–2025)
    Pioneered AI-integrated cyber-physical simulation of nonlinear SFSI systems impacting energy infrastructure, including facilities subjected to extreme loading. Developed NN-based surrogate models to represent complex nonlinear hysteretic soil behavior in real time, enabling efficient evaluation of coupled soil–foundation–structure response and informing design and resilience of critical energy systems.

    Nonlinear Systems, Smart Dampers, and Advanced Materials for Resilient Energy Infrastructure (2021–2024)
    Led research on nonlinear structural systems incorporating advanced materials and control devices, including magnetorheological (MR) dampers and seismic isolation technologies. Applied NN models within cyber-physical simulation to capture rate-dependent and nonlinear behavior, enabling real-time evaluation and enhanced performance of energy infrastructure systems subjected to dynamic and extreme loading conditions.

    Datasets or data infrastructure

    We seek industry partners to provide high-quality datasets and data infrastructure to support AI-enabled modeling of fluid–structure interaction in energy systems. Required data includes operating conditions, geometry, and performance data for reactor cooling components and data center cooling systems, including flow, pressure, temperature, and vibration response. Time-resolved data capturing VIV and thermal–hydraulic coupling is critical. Partners with secure data sharing and HPC-integrated infrastructure are desired for training and validating digital twin models.

    Subject matter expertise

    We seek industry partners with expertise in thermal–hydraulics, fluid–structure interaction (FSI), and cooling system design for energy applications, including nuclear reactors and data centers. Experience in vortex-induced vibration, flow-induced fatigue, and component reliability is critical. Partners with knowledge of system operation, instrumentation, and design/qualification requirements are desired to inform realistic conditions and support deployment of AI-enabled digital twins.

    Project Idea Summary

    Our project focuses on overcoming a critical bottleneck in fluid–structure interaction (FSI) modeling for energy systems, where conventional CFD methods are too computationally expensive for real-time analysis. We aim to develop an AI-enabled cyber-physical simulation framework that replaces CFD with neural network surrogate models embedded in real-time multi-physics cyber-physical simulation, enabling rapid, high-fidelity prediction of vortex-induced vibration, flow-induced fatigue, and thermal–hydraulic behavior.

    The focus is on cooling components in advanced nuclear reactors and high-density data center systems, where flow-induced phenomena drive reliability and performance limitations.

    We seek partners who can provide application-relevant datasets, system knowledge, and validation use cases, including operating conditions, component designs, and performance data. Collaboration will support co-development and validation of AI-enabled digital twins for real-time monitoring, design optimization, and improved system reliability.
