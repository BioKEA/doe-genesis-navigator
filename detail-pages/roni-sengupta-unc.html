    Introduction

    I am an Assistant Professor of Computer Science at UNC-Chapel Hill, specializing in 3D/4D Computer Vision, Generative AI, and Spatial AI. My lab bridges the gap between 2D visual observations and 3D physical realities. We build state-of-the-art AI models that capture realistic object appearance, complex deformations, and physical dynamics directly from limited visual data, such as standard monocular video.

    For the DOE Genesis Mission, our capabilities enable "hyper-realistic digital twins" and advanced spatial perception across several core domains:

    • Dynamic Digital Twins & Smart Infrastructure (Topics 1, 4, 13): Translating standard camera feeds into 4D digital twins that track equipment states, structural deformations, robot poses, and human workers in real-time for factory floors, nuclear facilities, and accelerators. 

    • 4D Physics & Fluid Dynamics (Topics 5, 15, 21): Using our efficient 4D reconstruction methods to extract 3D physical properties from 2D observational video. This acts as a fast, vision-based surrogate for complex simulations in fluid dynamics, plasma tracking, and atmospheric microphysics. 

    • Spatial AI for Autonomous Labs (Topic 11): Providing the real-time 3D spatial awareness necessary for embodied AI and robots to navigate and manipulate unstructured laboratory environments.

    I am seeking National Lab and Industry partners to collaborate on proposals, where 3D/4D visual perception and modeling accelerate AI for Science.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings
    AI models, tools, or software

    We offer state-of-the-art 3D/4D computer vision and spatial AI models, including Neural Radiance Fields, 3D Gaussian Splatting, Diffusion models, and Vision Language Models. 
    Our software extracts 3D geometry, material reflectance, and temporal dynamics directly from sparse 2D video. We also provide generative AI tools for synthetic data creation and inverse physics estimation, enabling the rapid creation of hyper-realistic, dynamic 4D digital twins from standard camera feeds. These tools are ideal for monitoring manufacturing, fluid dynamics, and autonomous lab robotics. 

    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    My SPIN Lab at UNC brings deep expertise at the intersection of Computer Vision, Machine Learning, and Computer Graphics. We specialize in solving Inverse Graphics and Inverse Physics problems—decomposing complex visual data into intrinsic physical components (shape, appearance, motion, deformation, and lighting). We have extensive experience training generative AI on sparse, noisy visual data to predict physical dynamics. Our expertise directly supports DOE goals in AI-driven smart manufacturing, fluid flow analysis, and spatial perception for embodied AI. 

    Subject matter expertise

    I offer domain expertise in 3D/4D Computer Vision, Neural Rendering, and Spatial & Physical Intelligence. With active NIH, Lenovo, NCDOT, and NCInnovation (supported by DoW) funding, my team builds 4D dynamic digital twins of highly deformable, complex environments from monocular video. I provide specialized knowledge in "Pixels to Physics" modeling—using AI to estimate physical properties, structural deformations, and fluid dynamics from visual sensors. This expertise is critical for building vision-based surrogates for physical simulations in energy and manufacturing domains.

    Relevant Projects

    Project 1: Real-Time 4D Digital Twins for Complex, Deformable Environments

    Scope: Supported by NIH Trailblazer and R21 awards, this project focuses on creating highly accurate, 4D dynamic digital twins of human airways from standard monocular endoscopy videos. The goal is to build generative AI simulators for robotic surgery and medical analysis.

    Achievements: We developed novel neural rendering and spatial AI techniques (e.g., NFL-BA, PPSNet, ProJo4D) to reconstruct textureless, highly deformable 3D environments in real-time. This work demonstrates the capability to extract complex 3D geometry, lighting, and temporal dynamics from highly constrained, sparse visual sensors. These foundational techniques are directly translatable to monitoring complex, dynamic industrial environments for the DOE—such as smart manufacturing floors, nuclear facilities, or autonomous laboratories—where tracking physical deformation and equipment states from camera feeds is critical.

    Project 2: Inverse Physics Estimation and Sparse-View 4D Reconstruction 
    Scope: This ongoing research thrust in the SPIN Lab (highlighted by our recent ProJo4D and GAINS frameworks) tackles "Inverse Physics" problems. The scope involves decomposing standard 2D video feeds into their intrinsic physical components: 3D geometry, motion, material reflectance, and underlying physical dynamics.

    Achievements: We successfully developed a scene representation and optimization/learning framework that achieves highly efficient 4D scene reconstruction from a single or  a few camera views. By integrating physics-informed constraints into 3D Gaussian Splatting and Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs), we enabled the extraction of physical properties directly from pixels. This achievement provides a fast, vision-based surrogate for computationally expensive 3D physical simulations, which is highly applicable to DOE challenges in fluid dynamics tracking, plasma monitoring, and material deformation analysis.

    Project 3: Generative AI and Synthetic Data for Critical Applications

    Scope: Supported by Lenovo & NCInnovation grants, this project develops generative AI pipelines to create high-fidelity synthetic data by precisely editing specific physical attributes, such as lighting, fluid/gas/sand interactions, and temporal aging, within existing visual data. A core focus is performing localized edits that strictly preserve the rest of the scene without introducing AI hallucinations.

    Achievements: We engineered several state-of-the-art frameworks for targeted generative editing. ScribbleLight & HarmoVid enable precise, user-guided relighting; Over++ seamlessly composites complex physical interactions (e.g., fluids, gas, sand) into videos; and MyTimeMachine & Aging Multiverse perform condition-aware temporal transformations. By isolating and editing only critical components while maintaining the integrity of the original environment, we can generate massive, highly reliable synthetic datasets. For the DOE, this capability is vital for simulating rare physical events (e.g., fluid leaks, thermal changes, material aging) in manufacturing or energy facilities, providing robust, hallucination-free training data for safety and control systems.

    Capabilities Seeking
    Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

    Training state-of-the-art 3D/4D generative models, Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs), and 3D Gaussian Splatting frameworks requires significant GPU compute. We are seeking partners with access to high-performance computing (HPC) clusters or DOE Leadership Computing Facilities to scale our inverse physics estimation and spatial AI models on massive, high-resolution scientific datasets.

    Datasets or data infrastructure

    Our 3D/4D vision models require observational data to reconstruct physical environments. We are seeking partners who can provide large-scale, multi-modal visual datasets—such as high-speed camera footage of fusion plasmas, X-ray micro-tomography of materials, fluid flow imaging, or multi-view camera feeds from manufacturing floors or laboratories. We will use this data to train and validate our vision-based digital twins and physics estimation models.

    Experimental or test facilities

    To validate our real-time 4D digital twins and spatial perception models, we are seeking access to DOE experimental testbeds. This includes smart manufacturing demonstration facilities, autonomous robotic laboratories, or energy infrastructure testbeds where we can deploy our camera-based AI systems to monitor equipment states, track physical deformations, and provide real-time feedback for autonomous control.

    Subject matter expertise

    While we provide the core AI and 3D vision methodology, we are seeking domain scientists (e.g., physicists, material scientists, fluid dynamicists, and nuclear engineers). We need subject matter experts to define the critical physical constraints of the target systems, guide the integration of governing physical laws into our generative models, and rigorously validate the scientific accuracy of our 3D/4D reconstructions. 

    Project Idea Summary

    We propose developing "Pixels-to-Physics" 4D digital twins and spatial AI systems that extract 3D geometry, material properties, and physical dynamics directly from sparse 2D visual data (e.g., monocular or multi-view video). By combining explicit 3D perception (NeRFs, 3D Gaussian Splatting) with physically-grounded generative editing, we can create dynamic, real-time virtual representations of complex environments.

    Our project ideas target several BAA Focus Areas:

    • Smart Manufacturing (1E) & Autonomous Power Plants (4B): Creating real-time, hyper-realistic 4D digital twins of factory floors or energy infrastructure to track equipment states, structural deformations, and human/robot interactions.

    • Autonomous Laboratories (11A): Providing robust 3D spatial perception and scene understanding for embodied AI and lab robotics navigating unstructured environments.

    • Fluid Flow (21) & Plasma Science (5G): Using inverse physics estimation to reconstruct 3D fluid or plasma dynamics from 2D observational video, acting as fast vision-based surrogates for complex physical simulations.

    What we seek: We are looking for National Lab or Industry partners with domain expertise (physics, materials, fluid dynamics, manufacturing). We seek access to experimental testbeds, HPC resources, and large-scale visual/observational datasets (e.g., high-speed camera feeds, X-ray imaging, or facility monitoring data) to train and validate our 3D/4D vision models.
