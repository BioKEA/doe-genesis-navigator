    Introduction

    Shashi Shekhar is a leading scholar of spatial computing, spatial data science, GeoAI and Geographic Information Systems (GIS). Contributions include scalable algorithms for eco-routing, evacuation route planning and spatial pattern (e.g., colocation) mining, along with an Encyclopedia of GIS, a Spatial Databases textbook, and a spatial computing book for professionals. Shashi is a McKnight Distinguished University Professor, a Distinguished University Teaching Professor, and an ADC Chair at the University of Minnesota. 

    Currently, he is serving as the Director of a National AI Research Institute, namely AI-LEAF, intersecting AI, Regenerative Agriculture and Sustainable Forestry, lead of the climate and sustainability group of the Computing Research Association (CRA) workgroup on socially responsible computing, a co-Editor-in-Chief of the Geo-Informatica journal (Springer), and an Associate Director of his college's Data Science Initiative, Earlier, he presented at a Congressional reception (2015), co-chaired IEEE Intl. Conf. on Data Mining (2025), SIAM Data Mining conference (2023, 2024), CRA Snowbird conference (2022), and served as the President of the University Consortium for GIS (UCGIS). He also served as a member of many National Academies' committees and the CRA board. Recognitions include IEEE-CS Technical Achievement Award, UCGIS Education Award, IEEE Fellow and AAAS Fellow.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings
    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    Traditional AI/ML performs poorly on geospatial data leading to costly salt-n-pepper map noise, and positional and prediction errors due to spatial autocorrelation and spatial variability, modifiable areal unit problem, etc.  To overcome these challenges, our group provides GeoAI methods for spatial classification and prediction (e.g., spatial auto-regression, spatial decision trees, spatial variability aware neural networks) as well as discovering spatial patterns such as hotspots (e.g., SaTScan, linear, arbitrary shapes), interactions (e.g., co-locations, tele-connections).

    Relevant Projects

    Ongoing Activities leading a national AI Institute: The AI-LEAF (National AI Institute for Land, Economy, Agriculture and Forestry) aims at accelerating adoption of resilient and sustainable land-management practices. These practices are of interest to farmers, foresters, companies, and policy-makers due to past experiences (e.g., Dust Bowl years) and improved understanding of ecosystem services. Currently, land-stewards are aware of many such practices and face a key decision: which practice should be used where, when and for how long? Challenges include the trade-offs between short-term and long-term economic and ecosystem considerations and confounding factors such as cropping, soil condition, and weather. To support such decisions via field-to-market decision support tools, AI-LEAF is developing novel AI-informed models to improve prediction accuracy, providing AI-driven higher resolution maps to improve data quality, and probing AI-guided surrogate models and multi-objective Pareto optimization algorithms to speed-up computational response time. However, traditional AI faces challenges such as out of sample prediction, hard constraints (e.g., conservation of mass or energy), etc. Thus, AI-LEAF is advancing AI by incorporating laws of nature to improve prediction out-of-sample, and combining learning and reasoning, etc. AI-LEAF is also expanding rural and urban AI workforce and catalyzing a cross-sector collaboration nexus.

    Recent Activities intersecting Generative AI and Science: Co-organized a 2025 workshop titled GenAI4Science: Integrating Scientific Knowledge into Generative AI (https://sites.google.com/umn.edu/genai4sc2025). Made an invited presentation and served on a panel in the 2026 AAAI Workshop on AI to Accelerate Science and Engineering. Served on a panel in IEEE International Conference on Data Mining (2025) exploring challenge and opportunities intersecting generative AI and scientific research. Published papers on use-inspired innovations intersecting diffusion models and geospatial problems such as trajectory generations.

    Earlier Activities on Evacuation Route Planning: Developed scalable capacity-constrained route planning (CCRP) algorithms for emergency preparation and response to move vulnerable population to safety in the event of natural or man-made disasters. Experiments and case studies showed that our algorithms were much faster than the competition such as microscopic traffic simulation (e.g., Wardrop equilibrium of commute traffic) and dynamic traffic assignment based on linear programming. Our algorithms were used for shelter allocation in Hajj (Mecca, Saudi Arabia) and metropolitan emergency management planning in Minneapolis/St. Paul metropolitan area. Consequently, it was recognized for significant impact on transportation by the Center for Transportation Studies at the Univ. of Minnesota. It was also presented at a Congressional breakfast on GIS and Homeland Security.
