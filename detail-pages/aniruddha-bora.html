    Introduction

    I am Aniruddha Bora, Assistant Professor of Computer Science at Texas State University and Director of the SPARKS Research Group (Scientific Prediction through AI Research, Knowledge & Simulation). My research focuses on physics-informed machine learning methods, including physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), neural operators, and symbolic physics discovery to accelerate scientific modelling across energy, climate, and materials science domains. I completed postdoctoral training under George Em Karniadakis at Brown University,  and maintain active research collaborations with DOE National Laboratories, including Argonne National Laboratory and Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL), as well as MIT Sea Grant. My recent work includes hybrid neural operator frameworks for bias correction in DOE's E3SM Earth system model, physics-informed approaches to thermal management in semiconductor devices, and offline reinforcement learning for energy-efficient maritime vessel routing. I am also developing novel frameworks for latent symbolic physics discovery and physics-grounded offline scientific intelligence that combine data-driven AI with principled physical reasoning.

    I am seeking interdisciplinary partnerships with National Laboratory scientists, industry collaborators, and academic teams to advance AI-accelerated discovery in energy systems, earth system modelling, and advanced materials, core priorities of the Genesis Mission

    Relevant Projects

    Project 1: Physics-Informed Offline Reinforcement Learning for Maritime Energy Optimization (PIER) Developed PIER, a physics-informed offline reinforcement learning framework for fuel-efficient maritime vessel routing. The system integrates real-world AIS shipping data, NOAA ocean current and wave reanalysis, and physics-based speed-loss models into an offline RL pipeline that learns near-optimal routing policies without costly online exploration. PIER eliminates catastrophic fuel waste by embedding physical constraints directly into the decision-making process. The framework demonstrated significant fuel savings and CO₂ emission reductions compared to conventional routing baselines. This work involved collaboration with MIT Sea Grant (Julie Chalfant, Chryssostomos Chryssostomidis). A provisional patent has been filed, and the manuscript is targeting Nature Machine Intelligence (submitted). The methodology generalizes to broader energy systems optimization where physics constraints, large observational datasets, and sequential decision-making intersect. (2 invited talks: UT Austin & MIT)

    Project 2: Neural Operator Frameworks for Online Bias Correction in DOE's E3SM Model Collaborated with Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL) to develop plug-in neural operator architectures for online bias correction in DOE's Energy Exascale Earth System Model (E3SMv2). Designed two novel architectures: Inception UNet (IUNet) and Multi-scale Multi-branch (M&M) — built on UNet backbones with Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) conditioning to incorporate geophysical context. A key finding was that architectural expressivity, not parameter count, drives performance: M&M achieved the highest pattern correlations (0.93 for zonal wind) and lowest errors while matching the baseline's parameter budget. Both architectures were successfully integrated online into EAMv2 via a PyTorch-Fortran interface, remaining numerically stable across 5-year simulations. Multiple publications resulted (3), including Geoscientific Model Development (2026). The framework demonstrates a practical pathway for retrofitting legacy Earth system models with expressive ML operators. (4 invited talks)

    Project 3: Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Semiconductor Thermal Management Developed ANN and PINN-based methodologies for thermal transport problems across micro- and nanoscale systems. Initial work addressed nanoscale heat conduction in gold thin films under ultrashort-pulsed laser irradiation, developing neural network methods for both parabolic and nonlocal two-temperature models that capture temporal and spatial nonlocal effects when characteristic length scales become comparable to heat carrier mean free paths. Two publications in International Journal of Heat and Mass Transfer (2021, 2022). Building on this foundation, subsequent work developed PINN-based methodologies for solving inverse thermal transport problems in multilayered MOSFET devices, critical components of Power Electronic Building Blocks (PEBBs). Designed a sequential layer-training algorithm to determine required coolant velocities given thermal boundary conditions across materials with different conductivities (aluminum, pyrolytic graphite sheets, stainless steel), addressing an ill-posed inverse problem difficult to solve with conventional methods. Together, these efforts demonstrate physics-informed AI's capability to accelerate thermal design in microelectronics and energy devices. (2 invited talks).

    Capabilities Seeking
    Datasets or data infrastructure

    Access to high-fidelity simulation outputs, experimental measurements, and curated scientific datasets from DOE National Laboratories and industry. Of particular interest are E3SM and other Earth system model outputs at multiple resolutions, experimental thermal and fluid dynamics data for validating physics-informed surrogates, energy grid operational data for optimization applications, and materials characterization datasets for training neural operators. Structured data pipelines and standardized formats that facilitate ML-ready ingestion would accelerate model development and benchmarking.

    Experimental or test facilities

    Access to laboratory and field experimental facilities for validating ML-predicted results against physical measurements. Relevant facilities include thermal test beds for semiconductor and microelectronics cooling validation, wind tunnels or flow facilities for CFD model benchmarking, materials characterization laboratories, and climate observation networks. Experimental validation is critical for establishing trust in physics-informed ML predictions and closing the loop between data-driven discovery and real-world deployment.

    Subject matter expertise

    Domain scientists and engineers in areas including fusion energy, advanced nuclear systems, grid modernization, advanced manufacturing, and Earth system science. Seeking collaborators who bring deep physical understanding of application-specific challenges where our physics-informed ML frameworks — PINNs, neural operators, symbolic discovery, offline RL — can accelerate modelling, design, and optimization workflows. Particularly interested in partnering with researchers at National Laboratories who maintain validated simulation codes and experimental programs.

    Project Idea Summary

    We develop physics-informed ML methods — neural operators, PINNs, and offline reinforcement learning — as plug-in surrogates and optimizers for DOE simulation workflows. Our primary focus areas are AI for Scientific Reasoning and Fluid Flow for Energy, with strong relevance to Materials Design, Microelectronics, Water for Energy, and HPC Code Development. Demonstrated capabilities include: (1) neural operators integrated online into DOE's E3SM climate model with PNNL, achieving stable multi-year bias correction; (2) inverse thermal solvers for semiconductor devices (MOSFETs/PEBBs) with MIT Sea Grant; and (3) offline RL for maritime fuel optimization (patent-pending). We have active collaborations with Argonne, PNNL, and MIT Sea Grant. We seek: National Lab scientists with production simulation codes and experimental facilities in energy, climate, or materials domains; industry partners offering GPU/HPC infrastructure and real-world validation datasets; and academic teams with complementary domain expertise in fusion, nuclear, grid, or materials science. We are also open to collaborations beyond these areas where computationally demanding physics simulations could benefit from our proven plug-in ML integration pipeline.
