Introduction

Limpossible Inc. is a deep-tech startup developing a physics-AI platform for quantitative material characterization using ultrasound wave physics. We address the empirical fallacy in material science by replacing heuristic-based measurement with rigorous physical reformulation grounded in wave mechanics. Our core technology builds on physics-based modeling for poroviscoelastic media, combined with a gradient-based optimization pipeline that has demonstrated 95% accuracy in 2D homogeneous material property estimation and 90% in 2D heterogeneous systems, with 3D extension as a key next milestone. We are currently developing AI models to replace computationally expensive wave simulators. Once achieved, this surrogate foundation will allow us to pursue AI-driven adaptive sensing strategies. Our custom broadband ultrasonic transducer array hardware, designed and fabricated in-house, enables broad-spectrum measurements across multiple frequency ranges. Primary application targets include smart non-destructive testing (NDT) for advanced manufacturing and composites, with a longer-term roadmap toward medical applications in biomechanics. We are seeking partnerships with DOE national laboratories or universities that can provide access to high-performance computing resources or expertise in neural operators.

Subject matter expertise

Limpossible Inc. brings deep expertise in wave mechanics through complex material systems, grounded in Biot theory for poroviscoelastic media, going beyond the linear elasticity assumptions of conventional ultrasonic characterization. Our expertise spans physical model formulation, numerical simulation, and inverse problem design for quantitative property estimation. We have developed and validated a gradient-based inversion pipeline capable of estimating elasticity, viscosity, and density in both homogeneous and heterogeneous 2D systems directly from ultrasound waveform data.

Relevant Projects

Project 1: Ocular Biomechanics and AI-Based Image Analysis (2021–2024) Conducted experimental and computational studies on ocular biomechanics at UCLA Stein Eye Institute. Applied image processing pipelines to clinical imaging data from OCT, OCTA, and MRI to quantify strain, displacement, and vascular changes in optic disc and peripapillary tissues during eye movement. Developed deep learning models for automated segmentation of extraocular muscles from orbital MRI, and for high-throughput analysis of droplet-based digital PCR images. This experience established expertise spanning continuum mechanics, clinical imaging analysis, and applied deep learning. This project provided the first mechanical engineering-based quantitative evidence for optic nerve tethering during horizontal eye rotation, demonstrating through strain analysis and finite element modeling that mechanical tension in the optic nerve directly drives deformation of the optic disc and peripapillary tissue during adduction. This work translated a clinically observed phenomenon into a rigorously validated biomechanical framework.

Project 2: High-Throughput Micro-Indentation and Inverse FEM-Based Mechanical Property Estimation of Ocular Tissues (2024–2025) Designed and executed a high-throughput micro-indentation experimental setup for spatial biomechanical characterization of animal sclera. Performed grid-based indentation across posterior scleral tissue using a commercial micro-indenter, collecting data curves across scleral surfaces. Developed an inverse finite element modeling pipeline using a poroviscohyperelastic constitutive model, executed on computing clusters, to estimate spatially resolved mechanical properties from indentation data. Demonstrated significant regional and/or depth-dependent stiffness variation for sclerae. This work established direct expertise in experimental mechanics, inverse problem formulation, and physics-based material property estimation.

Project 3: Limpossible Physics-AI Platform Development (2026–present) Leading end-to-end development of a quantitative material characterization platform grounded in wave physics. Reformulated Biot poroviscoelastic theory within an ultrasound wave propagation framework and implemented a full wave simulator, subsequently ported to GPU-parallelized computation for scalable simulation throughput. Designed custom broadband ultrasonic transducer arrays optimized for multi-frequency material characterization, covering the full hardware-to-inference stack. Built a gradient-based inversion pipeline achieving 95% accuracy in 2D homogeneous and 90% in 2D heterogeneous material property estimation. Currently developing AI surrogate models to replace the wave simulator for real-time inference. Filed two provisional patents covering the ultrasonic system architecture and property estimation methodology.

Project Idea Summary

Limpossible Inc. is developing a physics-AI platform for quantitative material characterization using ultrasound wave physics, targeting the challenge area of Reenvisioning Advanced Manufacturing and Industrial Productivity, specifically the Digitalization of Industrial Processes focus area.

The core challenge we address is that conventional ultrasonic non-destructive testing relies on linear elasticity assumptions and empirical calibration, which fundamentally limits measurement accuracy for complex materials such as composites, polymers, and biological tissues. Our approach replaces these heuristic frameworks with a rigorous physical reformulation grounded in Biot poroviscoelastic wave theory, enabling direct inference of mechanical properties from ultrasound waveform data without empirical correction factors.

Our current platform integrates a GPU-accelerated wave simulator with a gradient-based inversion pipeline, achieving 95% accuracy in homogeneous and 90% in heterogeneous 2D material property estimation. We are now developing AI models to replace the computationally expensive simulator. Once achieved, this surrogate foundation will support AI-driven adaptive sensing strategies that autonomously optimize measurement configuration for a given material system.

We are seeking a DOE national laboratory or academic partner that can provide access to high-performance computing infrastructure or expertise in neural operators.
