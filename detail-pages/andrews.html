Introduction

Adiabatic Reversible CMOS is a chip design approach that aims to recover and reuse energy that conventional logic normally dissipates as heat. Instead of treating each switching event as a one-way loss, it combines reversible logic with adiabatic energy flow, allowing more of the charge to remain within the system and contribute to powering later operations. This approach trades some extra circuit complexity and silicon area for significantly higher energy efficiency. Vaire Computing describes its work as “near-zero energy computing” built on this principle, aiming to reduce heat, power, and cooling constraints for AI and other compute-intensive workloads while remaining compatible with standard CMOS manufacturing and practical software stacks. On its website, Vaire presents this as a way to improve performance within real-world power limits rather than simply pushing conventional chips harder.

Relevant Publications

Sally Ward-Foxton, “Vaire Demos Energy Recovery With Reversible Computing Test Chip,” EE Times, September 3, 2025. This article reports that Vaire’s “Ice River” test chip demonstrated net energy recovery using an adiabatic resonator in 22 nm CMOS, and describes the company as a British reversible-computing startup founded in 2021.

Foundations of Generalized Reversible Computing (2017) — Michael P. Frank. Presented at RC 2017; this is one of his key papers on the logical and thermodynamic foundations of generalized reversible computing.

Relativized Separation of Reversible and Irreversible Space-Time Complexity Classes (2017) — Michael P. Frank, M. Josephine Ammer. This focuses on complexity-theoretic limits and overheads for reversible vs. irreversible computation.

Back to the Future: The Case for Reversible Computing (2018) — Michael P. Frank. This is a broad argument for why future computing efficiency will depend on reversible computing.

Generalized Reversible Computing (2018) — Michael P. Frank. This extends the theory, arguing that the usual bijective view of reversible logic is too narrow and that conditional reversibility is the right framework.

Reversible Computing with Fast, Fully Static, Fully Adiabatic CMOS (2020) — Michael P. Frank, Robert W. Brocato, Brian D. Tierney, Nancy A. Missert, Alexander H. Hsia. This is one of the more hardware-oriented papers, introducing the S2LAL CMOS logic family.

Quantum Foundations of Classical Reversible Computing (2021) — Michael P. Frank, Karpur Shukla. This connects classical reversible computing to non-equilibrium quantum thermodynamics and Landauer’s principle.

Michael P. Frank, “The Future of Computing Depends on Making It Reversible,” IEEE Spectrum, August 25, 2017. This is a verified IEEE Spectrum feature by Frank arguing that reversible computing is needed for major gains in energy efficiency.

Dina Genkina, “Reversible Computing Escapes the Lab in 2025,” IEEE Spectrum, January 2, 2025. This is not authored by Mike Frank, but it is directly about Frank’s work and his move to Vaire Computing, so it is a useful recent IEEE reference for the commercialization angle.

Karmela Padavic-Callaghan, “How to fix computing’s AI energy problem: run everything backwards,” New Scientist, December 2024. I could not open New Scientist directly because it is blocked/paywalled in search, but the title and its connection to Vaire Computing and Michael Frank are corroborated by public posts linking to the feature.

Relevant Projects

We are an early start-up with some unique capability.

The EE Times article says Vaire Computing has demonstrated what it describes as a major step toward practical reversible computing by showing net energy recovery on its first test chip, Ice River, built in 22 nm CMOS. The chip combines reversible logic with an adiabatic resonator that recovers and reuses charge that would normally be lost as heat, which Vaire argues could help break the growing link between compute performance and energy consumption. The article explains that Vaire’s strategy is not to ask developers to rewrite software, but instead to make reversibility largely transparent to existing workloads through hardware, while gradually improving performance, efficiency, and commercial viability for future AI and high-performance computing applications.
