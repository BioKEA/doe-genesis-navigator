    Introduction

    Professor Sekeh is an Associate Professor in the Computer Science Department of San Diego State University (SDSU). Prior to SDSU, she was an assistant professor of computer science at the University of Maine (UMaine) from 2019-2024. She joined UMaine from Michigan, where she was a postdoctoral researcher working with Alfred O. Hero. She held a CAPES-PNPD Post-Doctoral Fellow appointment with the Federal University of São Carlos (UFSCar), Brazil, in 2014 and 2015. She was a Visiting Scholar with the Polytechnic University of Turin, Turin, Italy, from 2011 to 2013. She was a recipient of the CAREER award and the CISCO research gift in 2022. Prof. Sekeh is the Director of the Sekeh Laboratory, and she received the Maine College of Engineering and Computing (MCEC) Early Career Research Award in 2023. Her research has been funded through NSF, Cisco, UMaine AI initiative, Maine Space Grant Consortium (MSGC)/NASA, and UMaine Space Initiative. 

    Professor Sekeh’s research interests are at the intersection of machine learning and computer vision, with a particular focus on the foundation of deep learning models and representation learning in feature map perception problems. Her primary focus is on design, improvement, and analysis of open-world learning techniques for AI-driven systems with emphasis on unknown detection, adaptation, efficiency, and robustness. Further, she works on continual learning methods, adversarial robustness, domain adaptation, and graph summarization.

    Project Idea Summary

    Embodied AI systems are crucial for robotics applications in the real world, spanning autonomous driving and delivery systems to robots in scientific laboratory environments. Traditional AI training paradigms, including gradient descent (GD), reinforcement learning (RL), and imitation learning (IL) face significant limitations in dynamic and complex environments. GD‑based models depend heavily on in‑distribution training data and often fail under distribution shifts. IL requires costly and extensive human demonstrations and generalizes poorly when encountering unseen conditions. RL methods, while powerful, are typically trained in simulation and suffer from the simulation‑to‑reality gap, arising from simulator artifacts and reward misspecification, which limits their reliability in real‑world deployments. Recently, the emergence of large-scale vision-language
    Foundation models have opened new avenues for embodied decision-making, yet effectively
    integrating these models into real-time robotic systems in complex environments and open-world settings introduces unique challenges for continual learning, efficient discovery platforms, and cost reduction, as well as real-time perception, action grounding, and path planning under uncertainty, and out-of-distribution (unseen) generalization for safely interacting with scientific laboratory instruments in unstructured scenes.
