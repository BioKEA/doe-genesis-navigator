Introduction

Juliana Freire is an Institute Professor at the Tandon School of Engineering and Professor of Computer Science and Data Science at New York University, where she co-directs the Visualization Imaging and Data Analysis (VIDA) Center. Her research develops methods and systems that enable a wide range of users to obtain trustworthy insights from data. It spans topics in large-scale data analysis and integration, visualization, AI/machine learning, provenance management, and information discovery, addressing application areas including AI for science, urban analytics, computational reproducibility, and biomedical data harmonization. She has co-authored over 250 papers, including 12 award winners and a Test of Time Award.  She served as elected chair of ACM SIGMOD and as a council member of the Computing Community Consortium (CCC), and was the NYU lead investigator for the Moore-Sloan Data Science Environment. She is a Fellow of the ACM and AAAS, and a winner of the ACM SIGMOD Contributions Award.  Her work has been supported by funding agencies and industry partners, including the National Science Foundation, DARPA, ARPA-H, the Department of Energy, the National Institutes of Health, and technology companies such as Google, Amazon, Microsoft Research, and IBM. Freire received her Ph.D. and M.Sc. degrees in computer science from the State University of New York at Stony Brook and her B.S. degree in computer science from the Federal University of Ceará in Brazil.

Project Idea Summary

The DOE complex holds the world's largest collection of scientific datasets, yet a key structural bottleneck prevents researchers from realizing their full value: relevant data is hard to find. Datasets are distributed across repositories, lack adequate descriptions, and cannot be queried in terms of scientific context — a researcher's question, experiment, or existing data. We have been systematically building toward a solution. Our work spans the full discovery stack: strategies to crawl and index datasets across disparate repositories; methods to infer dataset semantics and automatically generate rich textual descriptions searchable through keyword, natural language, and LLM-agent queries; techniques to surface structural relationships such as joinability, temporal overlap, and correlation; and Auctus, an open-source search and augmentation engine that unifies these capabilities in an expressive, interactive interface. Our goal is to extend this foundation into AI-powered data discovery agents that transform how scientists search for data. Rather than manually querying repositories, these agents will operate from scientific context — a researcher's question, experiment design, and existing datasets — and autonomously identify relevant data across heterogeneous repositories. Key challenges include usability, ensuring agents interpret scientific intent without burdening the researcher, and scalability to the large number of datasets in DOE repositories.
