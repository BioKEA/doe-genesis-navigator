Introduction

Sumit Kumar Jha, Ph.D. Professor, Department of Computer & Information Science & Engineering University of Florida

Sumit Kumar Jha is a Professor of Computer Science at the University of Florida, where his research addresses the interpretability, control, robustness, and efficiency of artificial intelligence systems. He earned his Ph.D. and M.S. in Computer Science from Carnegie Mellon University and holds a B.Tech. (Honors) in Computer Science and Engineering from the Indian Institute of Technology Kharagpur.

Prof. Jha's research program centers on building high-assurance and efficient AI at both the algorithmic and hardware levels.

Prof. Jha has served as lead PI on projects funded by DARPA, the National Science Foundation, the Department of Energy, the National Nuclear Security Administration through Oak Ridge National Laboratory, the Office of Naval Research, the Air Force Research Laboratory, and other federal agencies. He has held multiple summer faculty appointments with the Air Force Research Laboratory Information Directorate.  

His research has been published at premier venues including ICLR, IJCAI, ICML, NeurIPS, AAAI, CVPR, DAC, DATE, and ICCAD. He is a recipient of the Air Force Office of Scientific Research Young Investigator Program (AFOSR YIP) Award and has earned multiple best paper awards and nominations.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

Developer of tools for computing robust attributions of deep neural networks (IJCAI 2022, ICLR 2026). Research on novel attention mechanisms for efficient transformers (DAC 2024). Methods for jailbreak detection and biosafety evaluation of LLMs via nullspace steering (ICLR 2026) and symbolic mathematics (NeurIPS 2024 worksho). Techniques for machine unlearning in vision models using contrastive subnet erasure (CVPR 2026). Grammar-constrained approach for translating natural language to temporal logic (ICML 2025). AutoSafeCoder multi-agent framework for securing LLM code generation.

AI/ML/Data science expertise

Expertise in robust, interpretable, and controllable AI. Research spans explainability methods including Hessian-based token attribution for LLMs (ICLR 2026), integrated decision gradients (AAAI 2024), and neural stochastic differential equations for robust attributions (AAAI 2022). Adversarial robustness including protein folding networks and vision models. Hallucination detection and mitigation in LLMs using formal methods and entropy-based frameworks. Neuro-symbolic program synthesis combining LLMs with satisfiability solving. Applications to biology, medicine, material science, EM signals.

Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

Access to UF’s HiPerGator, described by UF as the fastest university-owned supercomputer in the United States. The fourth-generation upgrade includes an NVIDIA DGX B200 (Blackwell) SuperPOD comprising 63 DGX B200 nodes with a total of 504 NVIDIA B200 GPUs. UF reports the system was ranked No. 3 worldwide for AI “speed” on an MLCommons benchmark.

Subject matter expertise

Deep expertise in formal methods spanning two decades, from PhD work at Carnegie Mellon. Published with Turing Award recipient on model checking and hybrid systems verification to current research integrating formal methods with AI. Techniques for statistical model checking, Bayesian verification, satisfiability-based synthesis, and bounded model checking. Recent work applies formal methods to AI safety: dehallucinating LLMs via formal methods-guided prompting, grammar-constrained decoding for temporal logic (ICML 2025), and equivalence checking via SAT solving (ICCAD 2024)

Other expertise

Extensive research in emerging computing paradigms including flow-based computing on memristor crossbars and neuromorphic computing. Developed COMPACT, PATH, STREAM, LOGIC, and UpTime frameworks for nanoscale crossbar design, synthesis, and verification (DAC, ICCAD, IEEE TCAD). Work spans analog, digital, and hybrid in-memory computing for AI acceleration, 3D crossbar architectures for parallel graph computation, and fault-tolerant design. Six granted US patents in novel computing architectures. Research on automated hardware synthesis using LLMs with symbolic feedback.

Relevant Projects

Project 1: MEDAL through Autonomous Scientific Laboratories (DOE, $5M, 2023–2025) Lead PI across six institutions including Argonne National Laboratory. Scope: Design and automated control of autonomous scientific laboratories using AI and reinforcement learning. Achievements: Built multi-institution collaboration integrating AI-driven experiment design, robotic lab automation, and workforce development. Research produced publications on attribution-guided retrieval-augmented generation for scientific experiment design and image-based goal-conditioned reinforcement learning.

Project 2: AI-CRAFT (DARPA, $4.5M, 2023–2025) Lead PI across three institutions. Scope: Developing AI-powered tools for cybersecurity analysis, including automated code assurance and vulnerability detection using large language models. Achievements: Research led to a multi-agent framework for securing LLM-generated code through static analysis and fuzz testing and jailbreaking LLMs with symbolic mathematics as well as machine unlearning (NeurIPS workshops 2024, ICLR 2025, ICLR 2026, CVPR 2026). Produced techniques for co-synthesis of code and formal models using LLMs.

Project 3: FIRST-FM – Few-shot In-context Learning for Robust Semantic Transfer Using Multi-modal Foundation Models and Formal Methods (DARPA) Lead PI across two institutions. Scope: Developing methods for robust semantic transfer using foundation models guided by formal verification. Achievements: Research produced grammar-forced translation of natural language to temporal logic using LLMs (ICML 2025), neuro-symbolic program synthesis for multi-hop navigation (ICAA 2024), and zero-shot out-of-context object detection using foundation models (WACV 2025). Work advances the integration of formal methods with modern AI for assured autonomy applications.
