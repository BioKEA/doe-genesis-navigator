Introduction

The Louisville Automation & Robotics Research Institute (LARRI) is a multi-disciplinary team of engineering faculty, staff and students that have a combined expertise in the emerging research areas related to automation and robotics. This multi-disciplinary approach allows us to tackle large research projects and provide solutions for current and future manufacturing  and logistics challenges.

Our areas of research include: human-robot interaction, mobile manipulation, telepresence, automation and  robotics for industrial applications, networked robots and autonomous vehicles, and planning and control for mechatronic systems. 

LARRI has dedicated lab facilities on the UofL campus, including a 10,000 ft^2 research lab dedicated to human-robot collaboration, a 15,000 ft^2 dedicated to industrial robotics and automation, and a 10,000 ft^2 square dedicated to micro-nano manufacturing, precision robotics, and advanced electronic/MEMS packaging. 

LARRI is a major research center at UofL's speed school of engineering, with robotic assets exceeding $10M covering manipulation, mobility, drones, and industrial applications, and the only research facillity of its kind in the state of Kentucky. LARRI collaborates with two other major research centers on the UofL campus including the CONN Center for Renewable Energy, and the Micro Nano Technology Center.

We work routinely with industry, federal labs, and compete for federal grants, and we would love to partner for the Genesis Mission.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
AI models, tools, or software

1. Digital Twins. We have extensive experience creating digital twins of manufacturing environments populated with robots, automated equipment, and simulated people. Tools include: Unity, Gazebo, RoboDK, Webots. Tasks include navigation and manipulation.

2. Deep Learning. We have experience using synthetic data to train deep learning models in simulated environments. Examples include tools such as Yolo, RF-DETR, DexNet for segmenting objects from visual scenes. We have developed in-house libraries for robotics and multi-agent systems such as Neuroadaptive Controller and Factorization Machines.

AI/ML/Data science expertise

1. Expertise with deep neural networks such as Convolutional Neural Nets (CNN), Long - Short Term Memory (LSTM), Recurrent NNs (RNN), Temporal Convolutional NNs (TCN), and Transformers for video data analysis, continuous sensor data streaming. Features extracted are used for low-latency algorithms for robot navigation, manipulation or decision making on sensor data.

2. Modeling and identification for control of dynamical systems, including parametric and non-parametric modeling, Kalman filtering, model-predictive and optimal control, parametric optimization, and learning from demonstration.

Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

Zurada is a state-of-the-art High-Performance Computing (HPC) system that delivers over 1.6 PetaFLOPS of double-precision for AI, data analytics, and scientific workloads. Its architecture combines CPUs, GPUs, and large-memory servers:

86 CPU-only servers powered by AMD EPYC 9174F processors (4.10 GHz) for diverse workloads.

43 NVIDIA GPUs, including H200 Tensor Core and H100NVL models, optimized for large-scale AI trainng.

200 Gbps NDR Infiniband for low-latency, high-bandwidth communication.

5 PB DDN storage with Lustre parallel filesystem for rapid data access.

Experimental or test facilities

State of Art 35,000 ft2 Robotics and Automation Facility including:

1) 10,000 ft2 lab space dedicated to Human-Robot Collaboration. Facility has over 20 manipulators, drones, service robots, quadrupeds, and social robots. 

2) 15,000 ft2 lab space dedicated to Industrial Robotics and Automation. High bay facility has industrial robots, conveyors, and safety equipment.

3) 10,000 ft2 lab space dedicated to using precision robotics for micro-nano and material science discovery. Tools include Nexus, a custom robotic additive manufacturing and microassembly, nano-scale robotics, and 3D packaging.

Relevant Projects

1) KAMPERS Project: Dan Popa was co-PI for the 5-year, $20M Kentucky EPSCoR Track-1 project from NSF, “The Kentucky Advanced Manufacturing Partnership for Enhanced Robotics and Structures, (KAMPERS),” 2019-2024, developed structural electronic (SE) materials, produced 3D printed structures from biodegradable sources, and integrated sensing and other electronic functions into these structural elements to advance human-machine interfaces and robotics. In this project a team of 40 engineers, materials chemists, physicists, and synthetic biologists from eight academic institutions in Kentucky developed structurally enhanced robots that gather data from user feedback to achieve collaborative human-robot interfaces (CHRI).The team created integrated 3D-printed, modular, organic, robotic tactile sensors that have been mounted on robotic hardware, such as robot grips and end-effectors and published  over 20 journal and 30 conference papers. A significant outcome was the founding of the robotics institute at UofL (LARRI) to promote institutional transformation in research and education.

2) Nexus MRI Project: In this 4 year project from the National Science Foundation (NSF) (2018-2022, budget $2.3M), the research team led by Dan Popa developed the Nexus, a new instrument that enables robotic manufacturing of complex systems spanning micro to macro scales. This infrastructure will facilitate US researcher access to rapid prototyping of emerging microsystems, microrobots, wearable sensors, and multi-material nanoscale systems. This project paved the way for the physical realization of future advanced technology prototypes, such as hand-held microfactories for nanotechnology, the next generation soft robots, and higher performance solar energy conversion systems. The Nexus is an excellent capability for developing collaborative human-machine interfaces and can be used by Genesis teams to bring prototype research ideas to life and to combine AI and robotics for new material/device discovery and optimization. 

3) Multiscale NNCI Project: Dan Popa was co-PI for the Kentucky Multiscale NNCI node at UofL funded by the NSF over the last 10 years (2016-2025) provided state-of-the-art infrastructure and expertise in three dimensional fabrication/characterization across the length scale continuum from the nanoscale to the human meso/macro scale. The UofL NNCI node addresses the convergence of “nano” with emerging advanced manufacturing (AM) technologies, such as 3D micro-scale printing and direct-write aerosol jetting on non-conformal surfaces. KY Multiscale meets the pressing need to extend nanomanufacturing to three dimensions and integrate nanoscale devices with larger system. The NNCI network will extend technical and pedagogical findings of Genesis as it relates to fabrication, integration, and evaluation of dense sensor arrays for human emotion and activity measurement.

Project Idea Summary

For topic 11 (Advanced Robotics for Dynamic Laboratory Environments (ASCR)) and AI-Enabled Diagnostics and Remote Handling (FES),) we can provide telepresence robots to get started with physical manipulation of  samples in a lab environment, continuing to autonomous robots that can operate in these environments with little human supervision. Robots will be equipped with advanced tactile and vision feedback, and be interfaces to human operators through collaborative and adaptive user interfaces that operate over degraded networks.

For topic 1-D (Digitalization of Industrial Processes (ITO), and 1-E AI-Enabled Smart Manufacturing (AMMTO)) we can provide realistic, multi-agent digital twins, and recommender systems driven by custom AI algorithms and mobile robots for optimizing factory operations. Applications include smart logistics and inventory control, and plant-wide optimization.

For topic 11-A (AI-Enabled Materials Discovery, Development, and Qualification (AMMTO)) we can harness the AI tools and robots of our Nexus platform to provide a rapid prototyping and testing of new materials and devices in a closed-loop development and evaluation cycle.
