Introduction

a { text-decoration: none; color: #464feb; } tr th, tr td { border: 1px solid #e6e6e6; } tr th { background-color: #f5f5f5; }

Dr. Ashvini Chauhan is a Professor at Florida A&M University (FAMU) and a nationally recognized leader in environmental biotechnology, microbiome science, and AI‑enabled systems biology, with more than two decades of experience advancing DOE‑relevant research at the nexus of legacy waste remediation, critical materials recovery, and workforce development. His program has secured $19.5M+ in competitive funding from DOE, NSF, DoD, USDA, and national laboratories, with a strong emphasis on metagenomics, proteogenomics, and predictive modeling to engineer resilient microbial systems for complex, contaminated environments. As PI of the Collection of Uranium‑Resistant Microbial Assemblages (CURMA) and leader of AI‑integrated projects (including ALTEMIS‑aligned efforts), Dr. Chauhan brings GENESIS‑aligned expertise in Predictive Engineering of Microbial Communities, BioDesign, and AI‑Enabled Bioprocess Optimization. His work directly supports the DOE GENESIS mission by translating site‑adapted systems microbiology and AI into scalable solutions for uranium biorecovery, nuclear cleanup, and a diverse, AI‑ready STEM workforce.

Relevant Projects

1. AI‑Integrated Bioremediation and Monitoring of DOE‑Impacted Ecosystems (ALTEMIS‑Aligned)

Funding & Period: DOE / SRNL; 2025–2026
Role: Principal Investigator

Scope of Work

This project focuses on integrating microbial measurements, metagenomics, environmental sensor data, and AI‑driven analytics to understand and predict the fate and transport of contaminants (including uranium and heavy metals) in long‑term DOE‑impacted ecosystems at the Savannah River Site (SRS). The work leverages deep learning and digital‑twin concepts to connect biological indicators with geochemical master variables and environmental dynamics.

Key Achievements

Developed AI‑enabled frameworks linking microbial functional genes to contaminant attenuation trajectories.

Generated AI‑ready, multi‑omics datasets to support predictive engineering of microbial communities.

Established a scalable workflow that directly informs GENESIS objectives in predictive modeling, AI‑enabled bioprocess optimization, and site‑specific deployment.

2. Comparative Bioconversion and Biomineralization Strategies for Uranium Remediation

Funding & Period: DOE / SRNS; 2019–2024 (active into early 2021 within the 5‑year window)
Role: Principal Investigator

Scope of Work

This project evaluated microbially mediated uranium immobilization mechanisms, including bioreduction, biosorption, and phosphatase‑driven biomineralization, using batch and column studies with indigenous SRS microorganisms. The work integrated genomics, proteomics, and geochemistry to define molecular drivers of uranium sequestration.

Key Achievements

Identified key genetic and proteomic determinants (e.g., phosphatases, efflux systems, stress‑response proteins) that govern uranium stabilization.

Demonstrated sustained uranium immobilization under geochemically relevant conditions.

Produced foundational datasets now informing GENESIS synthetic consortium design and AI‑guided optimization strategies.

3. Metagenomics‑Guided Characterization of Uranium‑Resistant Microbial Communities

Funding & Period: DOE / SRNL; 2021–2024
Role: Principal Investigator

Scope of Work

This project applied shotgun metagenomics, metagenome‑assembled genomes (MAGs), and functional annotation to characterize bacterial and fungal communities from long‑term uranium‑ and heavy‑metal‑contaminated soils at SRS. The goal was to identify site‑adapted microbial traits relevant to remediation and resource recovery.

Key Achievements

Reconstructed high‑quality MAGs from uranium‑impacted environments, identifying hundreds of genes linked to metal resistance, redox control, and phosphorous metabolism.

Established the CURMA (Collection of Uranium‑Resistant Microbial Assemblages) as a biological asset for downstream bioengineering.

Directly advanced GENESIS priorities in BioDesign and Predictive Engineering of Microbial Communities.

(Optional) 4. Innovating Heavy‑Metal Bioremediation Using Phytotechnologies and Microbial Systems

Funding & Period: DOE; 2023–2025
Role: Principal Investigator

Scope of Work

This project investigated plant–microbe interactions and microbial indicators at SRS coal combustion product (CCP) impoundment sites to remediate heavy metals and radionuclides while restoring soil health.

Key Achievements

Demonstrated integrated phytotechnology–microbial remediation frameworks.

Delivered field‑validated biological indicators for monitoring ecosystem recovery.

Project Idea Summary

We are explicitly seeking a national laboratory–based partner with state‑of‑the‑art analytical and radiochemical capabilities to independently measure uranium concentration, isotopic composition, mineral phase, and impurity profiles of microbially recovered products. Specifically, we seek expertise and facilities for techniques such as ICP‑MS/ICP‑OES, XRD, SEM‑EDX, radiochemical separations, and isotopic assays, as well as experience working under DOE regulatory and QA/QC frameworks. A partner with capabilities in materials characterization, nuclear chemistry, or fuel‑cycle‑relevant analysis would provide critical validation of uranium purity and recovery efficiency, enabling us to close the loop between biological recovery, AI‑enabled optimization, and deployment‑ready nuclear materials workflows.

This collaboration is essential to translate biologically recovered uranium from a laboratory demonstration into a credible, measurable, and scalable DOE asset, directly supporting GENESIS goals in predictive engineering, bio‑design, and mission‑relevant scale‑up.
