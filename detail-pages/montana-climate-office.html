    Introduction

    The Montana Climate Office (MCO), based at the University of Montana, is a trusted source of climate data, monitoring, and decision‑support for the Northern Rockies and Great Plains. MCO’s mission is to transform complex environmental data into actionable information that supports resilience across energy, water, agriculture, ecosystems, and communities.

    Central to this mission is the Montana Mesonet, a growing network of high‑quality weather and soil monitoring stations that deliver real‑time observations critical for understanding climate extremes, hydrologic variability, and energy‑relevant weather risks. These observations form the foundation for advanced analytics and predictive modeling efforts.

    MCO is advancing AI and machine‑learning approaches to integrate observations, reanalysis products, and model output into scalable, cloud‑native data systems. These efforts emphasize transparent, reproducible workflows that enable next‑generation forecasting, climate risk assessment, and stress‑testing of coupled human–environment systems. By pairing dense observational networks with modern data science and physics‑informed ML, MCO supports DOE GENESIS priorities around predictive earth system science, infrastructure resilience, and the translation of climate intelligence into operational decision‑making.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings
    AI models, tools, or software

    The Montana Climate Office develops and applies AI‑enabled tools that integrate observations, reanalysis products, and model output to support climate and hydrologic decision‑making. Our work emphasizes physics‑informed machine learning, scalable analytics, and cloud‑native workflows that improve predictability of climate extremes, snowpack, and water availability. Tools are designed to be transparent, reproducible, and deployable across research and operational contexts.

    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    MCO staff bring deep expertise in applied machine learning, statistical modeling, and data science for earth system applications. This includes experience with physics‑informed ML, spatiotemporal modeling, uncertainty quantification, and the fusion of observational and model‑based datasets. Our team specializes in translating advanced analytics into usable products for forecasting, risk assessment, and scenario analysis.

    Datasets or data infrastructure

    MCO curates and manages high‑quality climate and hydrologic datasets, anchored by the Montana Mesonet and complemented by national observational networks, reanalysis products, and climate model outputs. We maintain cloud‑native data infrastructure optimized for large‑scale analysis, real‑time access, and interoperability, enabling efficient AI/ML workflows and long‑term data stewardship.

    Subject matter expertise

    MCO provides subject‑matter expertise spanning climate variability and change, extreme events, drought, snowpack and hydrology, and climate impacts on water, energy, agriculture, and ecosystems. Our work emphasizes coupled human–environment systems and the co‑production of actionable climate intelligence, supporting resilience planning and energy‑relevant decision‑making across the Northern Rockies and Great Plains.

    Relevant Projects

    Headwaters Hydrology Project (HHP): AI‑Driven Operational Hydrology (2021–Present)

    Scope of Work:
    The Headwaters Hydrology Project (HHP), led by the Montana Climate Office, develops and operates AI‑driven hydrologic prediction systems to improve drought and water‑supply assessment across Montana, the Upper Missouri River Basin (UMRB), and the contiguous United States. The project integrates large‑scale climate forcings, basin characteristics, and observed streamflow using machine‑learning and hybrid physics–ML approaches, with an emphasis on operational reliability and open data delivery.

    Achievements:
    HHP has produced an operational, ML‑based streamflow modeling system delivering daily estimates at HUC‑10 resolution, updated in near real time and distributed via open APIs and dashboards. Models demonstrate substantially improved skill relative to national hydrologic models and are used operationally within MCO’s UMRB Drought Dashboard to support state drought assessment and reporting. The project has also advanced scalable cyberinfrastructure for national‑scale hydrologic inference, enabling integration with soil moisture, snow, and climate indicators and supporting agency decision‑making in drought, water management, and ecosystem applications.

     

    Montana Mesonet Expansion and Operations (2019–Present)

    Scope of Work:
    The Montana Climate Office leads the design, deployment, and long‑term operation of the Montana Mesonet, a statewide network of weather, soil moisture, and snowpack monitoring stations. The project encompasses station installation, sensor calibration, quality control, cloud‑based data pipelines, and stakeholder engagement with state agencies, tribes, watershed groups, and federal partners.

    Achievements:
    The Mesonet has become a cornerstone of climate and hydrologic infrastructure in Montana, providing near–real‑time observations that support drought monitoring, flood forecasting, agriculture, and energy‑relevant weather risk analysis. Mesonet data are operationally used by NOAA’s National Weather Service, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, and the U.S. Drought Monitor. Public APIs and dashboards enable advanced analytics and AI/ML model development, and the network serves as a foundational data source for regional drought resilience initiatives.

     

    Northern Great Plains Regional Incubator for Drought Resiliency (NGP‑RIDR) (2023–Present)

    Scope of Work:
    MCO is a lead institution for the NSF‑funded NGP‑RIDR, coordinating state climate offices, mesonets, tribal colleges, federal agencies, and private‑sector partners across five Northern Great Plains states. The project focuses on building a modern, regionally coordinated drought early‑warning and innovation ecosystem.

    Achievements:
    NGP‑RIDR leverages a $64M+ federal investment in mesonet infrastructure (540 stations by 2027) to enable next‑generation drought assessment. MCO leads efforts to integrate dense observations with AI‑enabled soil moisture and streamflow models, address climate non‑stationarity in drought metrics, and co‑produce tools with tribal and agency partners. The project is establishing interoperable data‑model pipelines and coordination protocols that improve consistency, equity, and timeliness in drought response and disaster aid.

    Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

    The MCO operates data‑intensive, AI‑enabled climate and hydrologic services that require scalable computing and cloud infrastructure. As forecasting systems expand toward national‑scale, ensemble‑based hydropower and water‑energy applications, MCO seeks partnerships that provide elastic compute for AI training and inference, large‑scale cloud storage for observational and model archives, and production‑grade platforms for real‑time APIs, dashboards, and scenario analysis. These resources are essential to transition from research‑scale analytics to durable, operational decision support.

    Subject matter expertise

    Hydropower Systems Modeling and Operations Expertise

    MCO produces high‑skill hydrologic and climate predictions, but we do not operate or optimize hydropower systems. We seek partners with deep expertise in: Reservoir operations and dispatch modeling; Coupled water–energy system optimization;Rule curves, constraints, and real‑world operational decision processes. To translate AI‑driven hydrologic forecasts into actionable hydropower guidance, forecasts must be embedded within realistic operational models that reflect physical, regulatory, and market constraints.

    Project Idea Summary

    The Montana Climate Office is advancing a GENESIS project focused on improving hydropower resilience under increasing climate variability and non‑stationarity. Hydropower systems across the Northern Rockies and Great Plains are increasingly exposed to compound risks from snow drought, extreme precipitation, and shifting runoff timing, while existing decision frameworks often rely on stationary assumptions and limited observational data.

    This project will integrate dense ground observations from the Montana Mesonet with AI‑enabled hydrologic forecasting (e.g., streamflow, snowpack, soil moisture) to improve prediction of water availability and extremes relevant to hydropower operations. Our focus area is coupling next‑generation hydroclimate prediction with operational decision support for hydropower and water‑energy systems.

    We seek partners—national laboratories, utilities, and for‑profit companies—with expertise in hydropower operations, reservoir and energy system modeling, cloud‑scale computing, and decision analytics. Ideal partners will help embed climate and hydrologic forecasts into realistic operational models, support large‑scale ensemble simulation and uncertainty analysis, and translate forecast skill into actionable guidance for hydropower reliability, flexibility, and resilience.
