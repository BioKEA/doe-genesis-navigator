Introduction

The Quantum Biology Institute (QBI) is a U.S.-based 501(c)(3) nonprofit research laboratory in Los Angeles focused on determining whether quantum physical phenomena in biomolecules can persist long enough in living systems to affect cellular function. Our work centers on controlled experiments in microbial systems, including E. coli, to study how weak electromagnetic and hypomagnetic fields alter reactive oxygen species (ROS), protein fluorescence, and cellular phenotype.

Our research includes fluorescence-based characterization of proteins such as MagLOV2, with the broader goal of identifying additional biomolecular targets that respond to weak-field conditions. We operate an interdisciplinary laboratory spanning physics, engineering, and biology, and have built specialized tools including hypomagnetic chambers, custom bacterioscopes, and emerging quantum measurement platforms.

QBI generates structured, reproducible datasets and shares them openly to help establish a rigorous foundation for quantum biology. Within the Genesis Mission, we seek partners with strong AI and computational expertise to help model protein-level responses, connect molecular features to phenotype, and accelerate discovery of additional field-responsive biomolecules.

Offerings & Experience

Offerings
Datasets or data infrastructure

QBI generates structured, reproducible datasets on weak magnetic-field and hypomagnetic-field effects in biological systems, including fluorescence, ROS, growth, and phenotype readouts. We operate in an open-science model and share data to help build a rigorous foundation for quantum biology. Current datasets and workflows support discovery of field-responsive biomolecules and integration with AI-driven analysis.

Experimental or test facilities

QBI provides custom-built experimental infrastructure for studying magnetic-field effects in living systems, including hypomagnetic chambers, uniform-field coil systems, fluorescence imaging setups with magnetic control, and bacterioscope-based screening platforms. These facilities enable controlled, reproducible measurements of protein fluorescence, ROS-related biology, growth, and phenotype under defined field conditions.

Subject matter expertise

QBI offers expertise in quantum biology, biophysics, redox biology, microbiology, and fluorescence-based experimental design. We study how weak magnetic and hypomagnetic fields affect biomolecules and cells, with particular focus on ROS, magnetically responsive proteins, and experimental strategies to test whether quantum phenomena in biomolecules can become biologically relevant.

Relevant Projects

1) MagLOV2 magnetofluorescence in live bacterial cells (2025–2026)

Scope of work: QBI characterized the fluorescence response of the engineered protein MagLOV2 under controlled magnetic-field conditions in live bacterial cells, using custom magnetic-field-controlled fluorescence platforms. The work aimed to test whether the field dependence is consistent with a mechanistic framework relevant to quantum biology and to establish a tractable protein target for future higher-time-resolution measurements.

Achievements: QBI generated evidence that the magnetic field-dependent fluorescence of MagLOV2 in live bacterial cells is consistent with the radical pair mechanism. This project also helped validate QBI’s experimental workflow for screening field-responsive biomolecules and supported the transition from screening platforms toward quantum-enabled measurement platforms.

 

2) Weak magnetic field effects in Xenopus embryogenesis (2024–2025)

Scope of work: QBI carried out a large-scale study of Xenopus laevis embryos under hypomagnetic conditions to test whether removal of Earth’s magnetic field alters development. The project used controlled hypomagnetic environments to quantify physiological responses at organism scale while informing the search for underlying biomolecular mechanisms.

Achievements: QBI produced large-scale data showing that tadpole physiology responds measurably to the absence of Earth’s weak magnetic field, with accelerated embryogenesis reported under hypomagnetic conditions. This established one of QBI’s first flagship biological datasets and provided a strong experimental foundation for later target-identification work in more tractable systems.

 

3) Microbial screening platforms for field-responsive biomolecules (2024–ongoing)

Scope of work: QBI has built a staged experimental pipeline in microbial systems, especially E. coli, to identify proteins and pathways that respond to weak magnetic and hypomagnetic fields. This includes hypomagnetic chambers, incubator-compatible coil systems, magnetic-field-controlled fluorescence imaging, and bacterioscope/hypobacterioscope platforms.

Achievements: QBI has established the infrastructure needed to generate structured datasets on microbial growth, fluorescence, ROS-linked biology, and phenotype under defined magnetic conditions. Early work includes studying E. coli growth phases under hypomagnetic conditions and the use of screening systems to narrow the search for candidate field-responsive proteins. These platforms now position QBI to move from broad empirical screening toward AI-guided target discovery and follow-up quantum measurements.

We seek partners with AI models and software for multimodal biomolecular data integration, structure-function inference, protein/property prediction, and experimental data interpretation. Ideal capabilities include tools that can learn from fluorescence, phenotype, and other biological measurements to identify candidate field-responsive biomolecules and guide iterative experiments.

We seek collaborators with strong AI/ML and data science expertise to build workflows that demonstrate AI advantage in biomolecular discovery. Priority areas include machine learning for structure-function modeling, genotype/phenotype inference, feature extraction from experimental data, uncertainty-aware prediction, and model-guided prioritization of targets for follow-up experiments.

Project Idea Summary

We are interested in Challenge 2A and in a partnership that combines QBI’s experimental platforms with strong AI capabilities. QBI’s research plan is centered on identifying biomolecules whose fluorescence and biological function respond to weak magnetic or hypomagnetic fields, as a necessary step toward future quantum measurements in living systems. Our current work uses microbial systems, especially E. coli, together with custom hypomagnetic chambers. Additionally, we're using bacterioscopes and magnetic-field-controlled fluorescence platforms to study ROS, growth, phenotype, and magnetofluorescence in candidate proteins such as MagLOV2.

A key bottleneck is target discovery: without better ways to identify promising proteins and pathways, experimental screening becomes inefficient. We therefore seek a partner with AI/ML expertise and tools to help analyze multimodal experimental data, identify patterns across field-responsive genotypes and phenotypes, and prioritize new candidate proteins or systems for follow-up testing. The goal is to build a workflow in which AI guides biomolecular target selection and experiment design, while QBI provides the biological systems, instrumentation, and validation experiments. This aligns with the Genesis Mission’s emphasis on research workflows that incorporate AI and assess clear AI advantage.
