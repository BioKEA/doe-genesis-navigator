    Introduction

    Mayank Bakshi is an Assistant Professor in the School of Informatics, Computing, and Cyber Systems at Northern Arizona University and director of the LIMITS (Limits of Information and Trusted Distributed Systems) Lab. Mayank received his Ph.D. from Caltech in Electrical Engineering and brings over 15 years of experience, including as PI on funded research grants.

     

    His research establishes fundamental limits and develops practical algorithms for learning, inference, and optimization in networked systems facing adversarial uncertainty. His work integrates information theory, statistical inference, and distributed optimization to build resilient systems. Core technical expertise includes:

    • Distributed (Federated/Decentralized) learning under adversaries (Byzantine-robust and poisoning-resilient methods)

    • Adversarial sensing, robust detection, and hypothesis testing

    • Coding techniques for reliable and efficient distributed pipelines

    • Structured recovery (sparse and low-rank estimation)

    • Communication-efficient optimization over networks and physical-layer security

    For the DOE Genesis Partnership exchange, he is seeking collaborations that require efficient, secure, robust distributed architectures for network or AI systems. He aims to partner with researchers and domain experts to solve challenges involving decentralized data processing, high-dimensional statistics, resource-constrained environments, and critical systems vulnerable to adversarial disruptions.

    Offerings & Experience

    Offerings
    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    I offer theoretical and algorithmic expertise in distributed machine learning and inference, specifically focusing on environments constrained by dimensionality, communication bottlenecks, and adversarial behavior. My work develops Byzantine-robust and poisoning-resilient methods for distributed and federated learning, zero-order optimization techniques, and structured recovery algorithms for sparse and low-rank data. I provide capabilities to design, analyze, and deploy learning architectures that maintain statistical validity and operational efficiency despite adversarial attacks.

    Subject matter expertise

    My subject matter expertise centers on information theory, statistical signal processing, and networked systems. I apply error correction and coding theory to improve the reliability and efficiency of distributed computing and learning pipelines. I specialize in physical-layer security and adversarial sensing, formulating fundamental mathematical limits and practical detection mechanisms for systems facing worst-case channel or state variations. This expertise translates directly to building foundational resilience for decentralized networks and critical infrastructure.

    Relevant Projects

    1. Trustworthy Decentralized Learning under Adversarial Presence (VALID Framework)
    Scope: This project focused on designing learning algorithms that remain reliable even when some participants in a decentralized network behave maliciously. The emphasis was on providing adversarial detection at low overhead whereas standard adversarially robust methods often fail or become inefficient. The approach combined ideas from distributed optimization and statistical validation to ensure that model updates could be trusted.
    Achievements: The resulting VALID framework (ISIT 2024) provides provable guarantees against adversarial updates while maintaining scalability. The work is directly relevant to federated and edge learning systems where trust and reliability are central concerns.

    2. Byzantine-Resilient Federated Optimization for Large Models (Zero-Order Methods)
    This effort addressed the challenge of fine-tuning large models in federated settings where gradients are expensive or unavailable, and some clients may be adversarial. The project developed zero-order (gradient-free) optimization methods tailored for distributed environments with communication constraints.
    Achievements: The work (ES-FoMo III) introduced a Byzantine-resilient zero-order framework for federated fine-tuning of large models. It demonstrated that reliable training is possible with significantly reduced communication and without direct gradient access. This makes the approach practical for large-scale and resource-constrained deployments. This work has also resulted in a patent application (US and Europe). The European patent has already been approved in 2025.

    3. Adversarial Hypothesis Testing and Sequential Decision Systems
    This project examined how to perform reliable statistical decision-making when observations may be manipulated by an adversary. Both classical and sequential (online) settings were considered, with a focus on fundamental performance limits.
    Achievements: The work (ISIT 2024, 2023) established rigorous results for adversarial hypothesis testing, including sequential formulations and error exponents under worst-case conditions. These results provide a theoretical basis for designing detection and monitoring systems in adversarial environments.

    4. Robust Network Data Recovery and Scalable Inference

    This line of work developed methods for recovering network traffic data from incomplete and noisy measurements, a setting common in large-scale systems. The approach leveraged low-rank and sparse structures to make recovery computationally efficient.
    Achievements: The resulting algorithms (Neurocomputing 2022; Applied Mathematical Modelling 2021) were validated on real datasets and shown to perform well even under high loss rates. The work enables reliable network monitoring and integrates naturally with downstream distributed learning tasks.

    Capabilities Seeking
    AI models, tools, or software

    We are seeking partners with security-focused AI tools or software that can plug into HPC scientific workflows. Of particular interest are tools for adversarial testing, model hardening, provenance tracking, backdoor and evasion detection, and runtime monitoring during distributed training and inference. We are especially interested in software that can be evaluated on realistic DOE-style workflows rather than toy benchmarks.

    AI/ML/Data science expertise

    We are looking for collaborators with strong AI/ML expertise in adversarial machine learning, robust distributed optimization, AI security, foundation model fine-tuning, or scientific ML. Experience with poisoning, evasion, and backdoor attacks, defense benchmarking, and secure training or inference pipelines would be especially valuable. We also welcome partners who can help translate these methods into real scientific computing settings.

    Computing infrastructure or cloud resources

    We seek access to leadership-class HPC, secure cloud, or large-scale GPU infrastructure suitable for distributed training, attack simulation, and runtime monitoring. Useful capabilities include multi-node training, detailed telemetry or logging support, containerized deployment, and realistic networked environments for evaluating security-performance tradeoffs under strict compute and bandwidth constraints.

    Datasets or data infrastructure

    We are looking for partners who can provide representative scientific AI workloads, datasets, or data pipelines from DOE-relevant domains such as materials, fusion, climate, genomics, or related HPC science applications. We are particularly interested in curated data and workflow infrastructure that support provenance analysis, poisoning studies, benchmark creation, and evaluation under realistic distributed or multi-facility settings.

    Subject matter expertise

    We seek domain partners who understand how AI is actually used in scientific workflows and can help ground the work in realistic use cases. Expertise in scientific computing, HPC operations, research cybersecurity, or DOE mission applications would be valuable, especially where compromised models or data could affect downstream scientific conclusions, simulation results, or engineering decisions.

    Project Idea Summary

    Our planned project targets DOE Genesis Topic 20: Cybersecurity for AI-driven Science Workflows, specifically Focus Areas A, B, and C. The goal is to develop mathematically grounded, lightweight methods for securing AI used in large-scale scientific computing. The project combines three threads: realistic evasion and backdoor benchmark generation for DOE-relevant workflows; validated learning and provenance methods that can certify whether distributed training has been tampered with; and parameter-efficient, Byzantine-resilient fine-tuning methods for large scientific foundation models. We are seeking partners who can help us evaluate and harden these methods in realistic environments by contributing scientific AI workflows, relevant datasets, security tooling, domain expertise, or HPC infrastructure. Ideal partners would bring real use cases in areas such as fusion, materials, climate, genomics, or scientific foundation models, along with operational constraints that help move the work beyond toy settings and toward deployable tools.
