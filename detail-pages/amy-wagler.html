Introduction

I serve as the Director of Research Computing and Data Science at New Mexico State University. With a strong background in data science and statistics and with years of research experience at a R1 institution, I direct the research computing services housed under the NMSU Research Cores Program and coordinate with faculty and students around data science and computing. I have conducted research on graph theory modeling, with a focus on comparison of complex networks and error thresholding in these model settings. I have also worked on projects in generating synthetic data in biomedical studies, using approaches such as VAEs, GANs, and LLMs. My research trajectory also includes work in multiplicity corrections in high-dimensional settings and simultaneous inference in generalized linear model settings. This has application in any high-throughput data setting focused on model-based outcomes with complex dependency structures. Throughout my career, I have worked on many applied community-based projects in the data sciences, in areas such as biology, biomedicine, and education.

Applications for edge computing architectures

Project Idea Summary

I have interest in collaborating on the following projects and am open to inquiries regarding these projects.

Project 1: Edge systems, incorporating distributed highly dense sensors and instruments, create complex issues with latency, bandwidth to the core and demands on energy and environmental constraints and compute, due to the inconsistent power supplies, rugged compute conditions, and intermittent network availability encountered in practice. Despite the importance of edge compute performance, edge benchmarking remains fragmented, often microbenchmark‑focused, not workload‑realistic, rarely reproducible across platforms, and non-generalizable to evolving contexts. The impact of this is that edge compute systems are not optimized for their real-world environments and use, thus ultimately not serving the public good.

Project 2: This project explores a novel framework for synthetic tabular data generation using few-shot learning enhanced by advanced dependency‑aware representations. Traditional generative models often struggle to capture complex statistical relationships when limited real data are available. To address this challenge, we introduce new methods for depicting feature dependencies, combining structural encodings and compact dependency graphs, as auxiliary signals for few-shot learning models. These enriched representations guide the generative process, enabling the creation of high‑fidelity synthetic datasets that preserve statistical characteristics.
